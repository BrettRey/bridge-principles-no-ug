# Project Brief: Bridge Principles without UG

## Aim

Write a short paper responding to Reiss's claim that the postulate of Universal Grammar licenses crosslinguistic evidence. The useful wedge is that Reiss identifies a real problem: crosslinguistic evidence needs bridge principles. The disagreement is that the existence of that problem doesn't establish Chomskyan Universal Grammar as the only possible licence.

## Working Thesis

Crosslinguistic inference requires bridge principles; it doesn't require Chomskyan Universal Grammar. UG is one proposed licence for evidential travel. Others are available when projection targets, explanatory levels, profile support, stabilizers, and failure conditions are made explicit.

## Productive Concessions

First, theoretical linguistics can be empirical without being experimental, corpus-based, or statistical.

Second, categories such as word, sentence, negative-polarity item, segment, syllable, and interrogative element are not read directly from raw speech, text, or corpora.

Third, crosslinguistic comparison isn't automatic. French facts can help analyse English `any`, but only if there is an account of why the distinction travels.

These concessions should sharpen the disagreement. The paper isn't denying theory, abstraction, realism, or comparison. It denies the monopoly claim.

## Main Objection

Reiss moves among several claims:

1. Human languages are comparable.
2. Individual mental grammars are real objects.
3. The relevant primitives are innate and genetically specified.
4. Crosslinguistic evidence can bear on the analysis of particular languages.
5. Only UG licenses that evidential travel.
6. Non-UG linguists lack grounds for importing categories across languages.

The vulnerable step is the move from bridge principles to UG as the only possible bridge.

The internal flaw is fatal to the monopoly claim, not to Chomskyan inquiry as such. Reiss's UG postulate may define a research domain for his quoted `\enquote{linguists}`, but it doesn't by itself specify the projection target, transfer conditions, scope limits, or failure modes of a particular crosslinguistic inference. A bridge principle that cannot say when the evidence stops travelling is too permissive to support the exclusionary conclusion.

Projectibility-first linguistics can do more: it can distinguish mental-grammar, public-standard, corpus-distributional, typological, diachronic, institutional, and social-practical targets, then ask what profile supports each projection, what orders source and target properties, what stabilizes that co-availability, and what licenses inference across cases. Reiss's `\enquote{linguists}` can handle the I-language target on their own terms. They cannot infer from that success that the other targets are illegitimate.

## Boundary-Policing Point

The phrase `self-identified linguists` should not be the centre of the response, but it matters as evidence of boundary policing. The more important passage is the chapter's stipulation that, unless otherwise indicated, `linguists` means Chomsky and those who broadly share his philosophical stance.

In the paper, grant Reiss the quoted term: use `\enquote{linguists}` for Reiss's stipulated Chomskyan subkind. Then define `\term{linguists}` separately as a projectible category: researchers whose work is organized around systematic inquiry into human language phenomena. The profile licenses expectations about shared properties such as sustained engagement with language data, explicit methods for identifying and explaining patterns, accountability to disciplinary standards of evidence and argument, and results intended to bear on linguistic capacities, practices, systems, histories, or products.

That restriction may be harmless for reconstructing Chomskyan methodology. It isn't harmless if the chapter then draws conclusions about what `\term{linguists}` as such can do. The bridge-principle demand applies reflexively: Reiss needs a bridge from claims about his quoted `\enquote{linguists}` to claims about the larger field.

## Rhetorical Stance

Follow Rapaport's rules: reconstruct the target argument charitably, concede the strongest defensible points, quote accurately, and avoid cheap shots. After that, press the critique hard. The paper should expose the monopoly claim and the boundary-policing move as forcefully as the evidence allows.

## Positive Alternative

A projectibility account asks:

1. What is the projection target?
2. What property-and-relation pattern supports it?
3. Which diagnostics identify that pattern?
4. What ordering relation makes source and target properties co-available rather than accidental?
5. What stabilizes that co-availability across relevant contexts or timescales?
6. What scope conditions and failure modes mark its limits?

Following *Kinds as Projectibility Profiles*, reserve `profile` for the worldly property-and-relation pattern. Diagnostics, ordering relations, stabilizers, scope conditions, and demotion rules are part of the profile specification.

Support should be graded. A diagnostic cue identifies a candidate contrast but does not by itself license transfer. A scoped bridge states target, profile, diagnostics, ordering relation, scope, and defeaters. A robust bridge has survived independent cases, held-out contrasts, and known defeaters.

Projectibility should be framed as a second-order audit discipline, not as a rival language faculty. UG is one possible first-order filling of the bridge schema for I-language targets. The paper's claim is that the monopoly conclusion fails because non-genetic and non-UG fillings can also be evaluated by the same target/profile/diagnostics/order/stabilizer/scope/defeater standard.

Possible bridge principles include shared projection targets, ordering relations, stabilizing mechanisms, acquisition and transmission bottlenecks, diachronic pathways, recurrent communicative pressures, population-level conventions, typological recurrence, and independent linkage evidence. These supports are not interchangeable. A serious bridge principle should be able to localize, demote, or reject itself when the projected inference fails.

Useful prior-project imports, translated into the current projectibility-profile framing:

- *Naturalizing Typological Kinds*: use comparanda vs comparative concepts vs realizations. A comparative concept is the definition/diagnostic procedure; the comparandum is the target pattern under comparison; realizations are the language-internal forms/functions/categories that carry it. Cross-level couplings are bridge hypotheses, not identities.
- *Varieties as Conditioning Structure*: use varieties as public/distributional bridge targets. Register, dialect, and discourse community can be treated as participant expectation conditioned on situation, ascription, and identification. The projective test is whether conditioning state predicts likely forms and forms help infer conditioning state.

## Public and Distributed Objects

Public, community-level, and normatively regimented objects need not be compact individual substances to be scientifically tractable. They can be maintained, taken up, corrected, transmitted, institutionalized, and projectible. The reply to the Standard English/on-the-moon joke is that distributed norm-governed kinds are being misindividuated as medium-sized objects.

Use Standard written English as the compact worked example: target edited public standard; diagnostics style guides, correction, assessment, institutional documents, dictionaries, usage manuals, and edited corpora; stabilizers schooling, publishing, examinations, editing, reference works, and repeated public use; scope edited institutionally answerable prose; defeaters divergent institutional norms, stable acceptance of corrected forms, failure on held-out edited texts, or evidence that the feature tracks genre, medium, or identity rather than the standard.

## Worked Crosslinguistic Case

Use Reiss's French-English `any` example as a worked audit rather than an overclaimed proof. The target is not English as such but the analysis of English `any`: one lexical-semantic item or two. The comparandum is the functional contrast between polarity-dependent and free-choice uses; the language-internal realizations are English `any`, French `rien`, and French `n'importe quoi`. The French contrast is cue-level evidence showing that the distinction can be lexicalized separately. It becomes a scoped bridge only when English-internal diagnostics, an ordering relation, a stabilizer, scope, and defeaters are stated. This is the fatal pressure on Reiss's example: the bare UG postulate does not license the transfer by itself.

## Drafting Constraints

- Keep the paper a response with a positive alternative, not a general literature review.
- Do not make the rhetorical boundary-policing point do more work than the methodological argument.
- Avoid adding citations until keys and bibliographic details are verified against authoritative sources.
- Run a projectibility audit after the first serious draft.
- Run level/category error and terminological hygiene checks before preprint or journal submission.
