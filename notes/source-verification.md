## Barjamovic et al. 2019 (added 2026-07-01, projectibility analogy)

- Local source files: `literature/BCCH-BronzeAgeTrade-qje.pdf`, `literature/BCCH-BronzeAgeTrade-qje.md`, `literature/BCCH-BronzeAgeTrade-qje_images`.
- Bibliographic metadata verified from the PDF first page: "Trade, Merchants, and the Lost Cities of the Bronze Age," *The Quarterly Journal of Economics* 2019, 1455--1503, doi:10.1093/qje/qjz009, advance access 2019-03-27.
- Source anchors: abstract and introduction state that the authors analyze commercial records from nineteenth-century BCE Assyrian merchants and estimate a structural gravity model of Bronze Age long-distance trade; the data section states around 12,000 deciphered/edited texts from about 23,500 Kultepe/Kanesh texts, written by Assyrian merchants, families, and partners, dated 1930--1775 BCE; the introduction describes contracts, taxation, legal procedures, caravan protection, roads/bridges, and trading settlements.
- Projectibility anchors: the authors parse city co-mentions, explicitly exclude coincidental mentions, and isolate 198 tablets with cargo/merchant itineraries, yielding 227 itineraries and 391 directed travels between city pairs. They use the resulting structural gravity profile to estimate trade links and lost-city coordinates, then check estimates against historians' qualitative proposals and itinerary constraints. The results section gives distance elasticity 1.912 with standard error 0.189 and notes similarity to modern estimates.
- Interpretive limit: the paper supports an external analogy for projectibility under recurrent social-practical structure, not the claim that markets require no institutions, that ancient trade proves a specific political theory, or that commercial records explain grammar.

## Haspelmath 1997 (added 2026-06-29, horizontal indefinite-map sketch)

- `haspelmath1997` (*Indefinite Pronouns*, OUP) is in the central bib. OAPEN (`https://library.oapen.org/handle/20.500.12657/30819`) confirms the open-access book metadata, DOI `10.1093/oso/9780198235606.001.0001`, 1997 publication, 380 pages, and the book's claim to map functional/formative variation and constrained crosslinguistic diversity. Local copy found at `/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/Mendeley Desktop/Haspelmath/Unknown/Haspelmath - 1997 - Indefinite pronouns.pdf` (306-page local manuscript/author PDF; NOT copied into this project). Direct anchors from the local PDF: the overview on PDF pages 2--5 lists the nine functions used for crosslinguistic comparison, including specific known/unknown, irrealis, questions, conditionals, comparison, direct and indirect negation, and free choice; it also says the negative-polarity label is too crude for the observed distributional patterns. Figure 1 gives the implicational map and states the adjacency requirement: an indefinite-pronoun series covers adjacent functions on the map; English `any` covers functions (4)--(9), while Russian `-nibud'` covers (3)--(5). Chapter 4, around internal pp. 80--84/PDF extraction pages 60--70, repeats the map as Figure 8, gives the English distribution in Figure 9, and states that the 40-language sample has no counterexample to the implicational map and conforms to the adjacency requirement. This verifies the current `main.tex` claim that indefinite-reference contexts can be mapped as constrained semantic regions rather than arbitrary language-particular imports. Exact OUP printed pagination should be reconciled only if adding page-specific citations.
- Terminological note (Brett, 2026-06-29): the sketch deliberately frames the grouping as a functional comparative concept, NOT a syntactic category of pronoun, to avoid reifying "indefinite pronoun" as a category. This is consistent with Haspelmath's own comparative-concepts method and with the paper's category/function discipline.
