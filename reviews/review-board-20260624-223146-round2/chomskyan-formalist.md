## Review: *Bridge Principles without UG* (Chomskyan-formalist reviewer)

**Charitable preamble.** The paper does the hard thing first: it grants Reiss the methodological burden in full (§2, "What Reiss Gets Right"), grants the stipulative definition of "linguists" (§4, line 63), and repeatedly protects Chomskyan inquiry from the critique ("fatal to the monopoly claim, not to Chomskyan inquiry as such," §3 line 52; "UG can remain a candidate bridge," §5 line 93). That is not an anti-UG polemic, and I want to say so plainly. I also learned something usable from it: the support-grade taxonomy (diagnostic cue / scoped bridge / robust bridge, §5 line 79) and the comparandum/realization discipline (§5 line 85) are a genuine tightening of how crosslinguistic transfer should be licensed, and the explanatory-level discipline (§7 line 117) is exactly the right hygiene. A Chomskyan can use this machinery without converting.

### 1. One-sentence summary of the main claim
Crosslinguistic inference requires *some* bridge principle but not specifically Chomskyan UG, because a projectibility account (projection target, profile, diagnostics, ordering relation, stabilizers, scope, defeaters) can license accountable evidential travel and supports a pluralist realism about linguistic objects.

### 2. Strengths
- **The concessions are real and load the dice against the author, not Reiss.** §2 and the opening of §3 give away theory-ladenness, armchair empiricism, the warrant requirement, and UG's content-bearing power. By §3 line 47 the disagreement has been narrowed to one proposition (monopoly), which is the honest place to fight.
- **The support-grade taxonomy and comparandum/realization split (§5, lines 79–87) are a contribution in their own right.** "A serious bridge principle should be able to demote itself" (§5 line 93) is a good and falsifiable-feeling norm. This is the part a formalist would cite approvingly.
- **Level-discipline in §7 (line 117) is clean.** Separating I-language, public-standard, corpus, and typological questions is the correct response to a category dispute, and it is executed without sliding between levels.

### 3. Remaining weaknesses (specific, actionable)
- **The one worked bridge does not discriminate between projectibility and UG; it redescribes a case UG already handles (§6, lines 95–107).** The `any` analysis is maximally congenial to UG: one phonological form realizing two lexical-semantic items, a covert contrast surfacing overtly elsewhere (the move the paper itself lists as Chomskyan at §3 line 57). Worse for the thesis, the profile is defined as "the recurring contrast between items dependent on licensing environments and items associated with free-choice interpretation" (line 99), and *recurring across languages* is precisely what a Chomskyan calls a (semantic) universal. The paper has conceded at line 87 that comparative concepts give only "accountable comparison rather than importation," so the entire defeat of the monopoly claim now rests on the profile licensing genuine transfer in a demonstrably non-UG way, and the single profile offered leans on a cross-linguistically stable NPI/free-choice distinction. **Action:** supply a *discriminating* case, one where the UG license and the projectibility license give different verdicts (UG over-licenses and the profile correctly demotes, or a legitimate transfer UG cannot underwrite), or argue head-on why a recurring crosslinguistic semantic contrast is not just a universal under a new name.
- **Scope slippage between "crosslinguistic evidence" and "linguistic ontology" (§7 lines 109–127 vs. the thesis at §1 lines 29–31).** Reiss's monopoly claim, as quoted (line 31), is about *evidential travel across languages*. But the longest positive section is about public/standard objects and pluralist realism, which is a different dispute (what counts as a legitimate linguistic *object*), and the abstract closes on "pluralist realism about linguistic targets" (line 22), signaling the drift. Standard English versus a child's speech (§7 line 125) is not a case of one language's data bearing on another's analysis. **Action:** either narrow the thesis to evidential travel and tie §7 explicitly back to it, or broaden the title/framing to ontology and present the monopoly critique as a sub-case. Right now the strongest material answers a claim Reiss may not be making.
- **Two charity lapses a Chomskyan will seize on.** (a) §7 line 115 frames the public-language critique as "jokes" resting on "misindividuation" ("travel to the moon with Neil Armstrong"). The serious version, that there may be no coherently individuated object there suitable for a science of mind (Chomsky's E-language critique) **[external; needs source verification: Chomsky 1986, *Knowledge of Language*]**, is not a joke and is not answered by asserting the standard is "norm-governed, historically maintained." (b) §4 (lines 61–71) leans on the typographic contrast between "linguists" and `\term{linguists}`; to a formalist this reads as wordplay rather than argument, since Reiss has *stipulated* his domain and is making a conditional claim about category-importation rights ("Anyone who denies UG has no grounds...," line 31), not projecting onto corpus linguists. **Action:** quote the specific Reiss sentences where the stipulated group is actually treated as the whole field, and rebut the category-importation conditional directly; drop the device if the slide can't be exhibited textually.

### 4. Does the revised French-English `any` case fix the earlier abstraction problem?
**Yes on the original problem, with one residual gap.** The example is no longer discussed at the level of the framework alone: §6 now instantiates target, source features, profile, diagnostics, ordering relation, stabilizers, scope, and defeaters on a specific contrast, and it names real defeaters (negative concord, French dialect variation, the written/spoken mismatch, lines 105). That is a clear advance and resolves the "all method, no instance" complaint. The residual gap is one level finer: the section *lists* diagnostics ("downward-entailing contexts, negation, questions, conditionals," line 101) but never *runs* them. There is not a single glossed French example, nor a starred/unstarred English paradigm, anywhere in the section. For NPI licensing, the textbook empirical domain **[external; needs source verification: Klima 1964; Ladusaw 1979; Kadmon & Landman 1993]**, a "worked bridge" with no data is still abstract about the very thing it claims to work. So: yes, the method is now concrete; the linguistics is not yet on the page.

### 5. What would make this polished rather than merely promising
- **Put data in §6.** Two or three glossed French examples (`rien` vs. `n'importe quoi` in sentence frames) and a small English paradigm with judgments, source-grounded, using `langsci-gb4e` `\ea`. This is the single largest lift and would convert the section from a description of a bridge into a bridge.
- **Find the crucial case, not a compatible one.** The thesis needs one example where projectibility and UG come apart in their verdicts. As written, every worked case is over-determined (both stories predict the same analysis), which is consistent with Reiss rather than against him.
- **Repair the charity in §7 and §4** as above; engage the individuation worry at its strongest, since the paper's whole method is "name the level and meet the burden," and it should meet Reiss's burden too.
- **Decide the paper's scope** (evidential travel vs. ontology) and align title, abstract, and §7 to it.
- **Reconsider the Newton analogy (§3 line 55).** It is double-edged: substantive UG (locality, islands, parametric clustering) *does* generate equations, measures, and counterfactual expectations, which is the property the analogy credits to gravitation. Either show why substantive UG's predictions don't rescue it here, or cut the analogy.

### 6. Verdict
**Revise & Resubmit.** The concessions and the bridge-grading machinery are strong enough to publish, but the monopoly critique is not yet earned: the one worked example redescribes a UG-friendly analysis instead of defeating it, and the richest section answers an ontology question the title doesn't pose.
