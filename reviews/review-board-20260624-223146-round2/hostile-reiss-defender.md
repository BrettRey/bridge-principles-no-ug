## Review: *Bridge Principles without UG*

Reviewer stance, as requested: sympathetic to Reiss, sceptical of public-language ontology, hostile but fair. I read `main.tex` and the project `CLAUDE.md`; I did not look at `reviews/`. A note on grounding up front: the Reiss quotations (intro, "allows data from each language to bear..." and "Anyone who denies UG has no grounds...") and the `sec. 5.3.3` locator are doing argumentative work and I can't verify them from the draft. Same for the Stainton, Santana, Pullum, Haspelmath, and Croft attributions. **All of these need source verification before submission**; I flag them but do not assume they are wrong.

### 1. One-sentence summary of the main claim

Crosslinguistic evidential transfer requires *some* bridge principle but not specifically Chomskyan UG, because a projectibility schema (target, profile, diagnostics, ordering relation, stabilizers, scope, defeaters) can license accountable comparison and a graded, defeasible warrant for transfer, leaving UG as one candidate bridge for I-language targets rather than the condition of possibility for linguistic comparison.

### 2. Strengths

- **The concession structure is honest, not strategic throat-clearing.** Section 2 and the repeated "UG can be a candidate bridge where the target is an individual mental grammar" (lines 35, 45, 93, 107) genuinely narrow the dispute. This is not a strawman of Reiss, and it keeps the paper from collapsing into the anti-UG polemic the project frame warns against.
- **The non sequitur diagnosis is crisp and correct.** Section 3 (line 51) lays out five claims and shows the exclusionary one doesn't follow from "bridges are needed" plus "UG bridges I-language successfully." Reiss does need an extra premise for the monopoly step, and naming that gap is a real contribution.
- **Two methodological levers are worth keeping.** The support grades (cue / scoped bridge / robust bridge, line 79) and especially the demand that a serious bridge be able to *demote itself* (line 93) give the pluralism teeth. Turning that same discipline reflexively on the public-language objects (line 127) is principled and even-handed: it stops the alternative from being a permission slip. I'd want to borrow the "a bridge must specify its own failure conditions" criterion myself.

### 3. Remaining weaknesses

**(a) The worked example demotes the crosslinguistic evidence, so it doesn't exhibit a non-UG *crosslinguistic* bridge (Section 6).** By the paper's own taxonomy, a "diagnostic cue... doesn't by itself license transfer" (line 79). At line 101 the French translation is explicitly called a diagnostic cue. So what warrants the two-*any* analysis? The English-internal diagnostics (downward-entailing contexts vs free-choice readings) plus the "profile." The crosslinguistic part licenses nothing on its own. Trace the stabilizers at line 103: "recurrence of the contrast across environments" and "independent English diagnostics" are English-internal, and "overtly split realizations in other languages" just restates that the pattern recurs crosslinguistically. The one place the paper claims to *do* the work rather than describe its form ends up resolving Reiss's puzzle (why does French bear on English?) by making the French data heuristic and the licensing language-internal. *Action:* either find a case where the crosslinguistic evidence is non-eliminable, or name and defend the typological recurrence of the NPI/free-choice split as the bridge (it's a functional-typological universal tendency, a respectable non-genetic bridge) instead of burying it in a six-item stabilizer list.

**(b) The framework renames the bridge problem at exactly the point it claims to solve it.** Projectibility, as presented, is the *form* any bridge principle must take. UG is a first-order *filling* of that form, and the paper twice concedes UG can fill it (lines 35, 107). Providing the schema therefore doesn't defeat a monopoly claim about *fillings*; it reformats it. Reiss can grant every slot and still say: "for transfer of a formal analysis across unrelated languages, the only thing that fills the stabilizer/ordering slot is a shared endowment." The paper's answer is the stabilizer list at line 91, but that list either contains rival *universalisms* (processing bias, acquisition pressure, communicative recurrence, which Reiss can welcome or dismiss as under-specified, not escapes from universals) or lineage-internal facts (diachronic pathway, institutional maintenance, which don't obviously license travel across *unrelated* languages). *Action:* own the schema/instance relation explicitly. State that projectibility is second-order, that UG is one first-order filling, and that the thesis is "non-genetic fillings exist, here is one defended in full." Pick one stabilizer and make it explain co-availability for a real case, as line 91 demands but the draft never delivers.

**(c) The Newton analogy concedes the universalist intuition (line 55).** Universal gravitation licenses transfer across apples, tides, and planets *because it is a substantive universal law with equations and failure conditions*. The natural Reiss reply: "Right, and UG is the linguistic universal that does the analogous grouping; your real complaint is that UG hasn't earned its equations yet, not that you don't need a universal." As written, the analogy supports "you need a universal, and UG isn't a good enough one," which is a weaker and more Reiss-friendly conclusion than "bridge principles without UG." *Action:* either develop the disanalogy (UG as a not-yet-quantified gravitation sketch) or cut it.

Minor but worth fixing: the load-bearing vocabulary (*profile*, *profile specification*, *ordering relation*, support grades) leans on `reynolds2026kindsProjectibilityProfiles` and isn't self-contained; a reader who hasn't read the companion paper won't know what an "ordering relation" is or why co-availability needs an ordering (lines 75, 89). And the title/abstract oversell the crosslinguistic payoff: the paper's actual center of gravity is ontological pluralism (Sections 4, 7), since the only concrete crosslinguistic case is the demoted *any* example.

### 4. Does the Standard Written English case make public/distributed objects projectible, or is it still analogy?

**Mostly still analogy, with a thin genuine residue the apparatus doesn't earn.** The giveaway is the ordering relation at line 123: "correction, publication, assessment, and uptake *partly make* the standard whose future behaviour they help predict." That's constitutive, not predictive. In a genuine projectible kind the stabilizing mechanism is independent of our classifying practice; here target (an edited standard), diagnostics (school assessment, editorial intervention), stabilizers (schooling, examination, editing), and the ordering relation (correction, assessment) all collapse into one thing, the institutional practice of correction. When every slot is filled by the same fact, "projectible profile" redescribes a norm rather than exhibiting a property-cluster that supports ampliative, cross-feature projection. The interesting Goodmanian case is "observe A, predict unobserved B via a hidden common cause"; here A and B are both "conforms to the norm" and the common cause is the norm plus its enforcement, which is close to tautologous.

In fairness, the residue is real: the defeaters at line 123 ("a supposed standard feature tracks genre, medium, or identity rather than the standard"; "stable acceptance of a formerly corrected form") carry genuine empirical risk. You can be wrong about which features belong to the standard, and norm change can surprise you. So it isn't pure fiat. But that residue is ordinary sociolinguistics (variationist work on norm uptake and change, usage-tracking), and it doesn't need the profile machinery to be studied. So on this object the framework relabels rather than predicts. And structurally, Section 7 answers a *different* Reiss jab (the "Standard English can't go to the moon" joke) than Sections 5 to 6: it defends public-object *realism*, but never shows public objects *participating in a crosslinguistic bridge*. The paper asserts "the same discipline" (line 127); it hasn't shown the two arguments are one.

### 5. What would make this polished rather than promising

- One fully worked crosslinguistic transfer in which the crosslinguistic evidence is non-eliminable and a single named stabilizer is defended as explaining co-availability. The *any* case is the centerpiece and currently undercuts the thesis; fixing it is the difference between promising and done.
- Explicit ownership of the schema/instance relation, converting weakness (b) into a clarified thesis rather than a vulnerability.
- Self-contained definitions of *profile*, *profile specification*, *ordering relation*, and the support grades, so the paper doesn't presuppose the companion.
- Either separate the crosslinguistic-bridge argument from the public-object-realism argument, or actually theorize their link instead of asserting "the same discipline."
- Verify the Reiss quotations and the `5.3.3` locator verbatim, and the Stainton/Santana/Pullum/Haspelmath/Croft attributions. (Flagged, not assumed wrong.)
- Tighten or drop the Newton analogy.

### 6. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The logical diagnosis (the monopoly claim is a non sequitur) is sound and publishable, and the concessions are honest, but the positive program is schematized rather than demonstrated: the one worked case demotes the crosslinguistic evidence to a cue by the paper's own taxonomy, and the public-object section relabels a self-maintaining norm in kind-vocabulary. Fixable, not yet finished.
