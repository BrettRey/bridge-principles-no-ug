I've read `main.tex` (143 lines) and the project `CLAUDE.md`. Here's my review as a typologist and methodology reviewer. I've followed Rapoport: charitable reconstruction first, then the sharp points. Where I bring in scholarship beyond the draft, I flag it for source verification.

## 1. One-sentence summary of the main claim

Crosslinguistic evidence does require a bridge principle (Reiss is right about the burden), but Chomskyan UG is only one candidate bridge, and a projectibility schema (target / profile / diagnostics / ordering relation / stabilizers / scope / defeaters, with graded support) supplies a non-UG discipline that licenses evidential travel without importing categories.

## 2. Strengths

- **The comparison-vs-transfer distinction (Sec 5, line 87) is the paper's real contribution and it correctly diagnoses Reiss's conflation.** "Comparative concepts license accountable comparison rather than importation... Evidential travel begins only when the comparative setup is embedded in a profile with projection, support, scope, and defeaters." This cleanly separates "we may compare A and B" from "the analysis of A is evidence about B," which is exactly the equivocation Reiss's monopoly claim trades on. It is the single most useful sentence in the draft.

- **The graded-support notion (line 79: diagnostic cue < scoped bridge < robust bridge) does genuine anti-permissiveness work.** The paper's own worry, that a bridge can become "an all-purpose permission slip," is the right worry against a bare UG postulate, and the grading is a substantive epistemic answer, not relabeling. The companion idea that a serious bridge "should be able to demote itself" (line 93) is a nice inversion and worth keeping front and center.

- **The reflexive turn in Sec 4 is the strongest part of the negative argument and it stays methodological, not personal.** Applying the bridge-principle demand back to Reiss's own slide from "linguists" (the stipulated Chomskyan group) to *linguists* (the field) (lines 67-69) shows that Reiss needs a bridge principle to license his own exclusion. Enlisting Haspelmath and Croft (lines 81-83) as witnesses that respectable non-UG crosslinguistic discipline already exists is the right choice of allies.

## 3. Remaining weaknesses (specific, actionable)

- **The worked "any" case imports a contested comparative concept as its "profile," and the section never runs the English-internal argument that is supposed to block illegitimate importation (Sec 6, lines 95-107).** The profile is defined as "the recurring contrast between items dependent on licensing environments and items associated with free-choice or modal-like interpretation" (line 99), i.e., the NPI-vs-free-choice distinction. That distinction is itself a theory-laden comparative concept (the draft even lists "negative-polarity item" at line 41 as a category that is *not* read off the signal). The residual risk is precise: a two-pole *functional* comparandum is legitimate and Haspelmathian, but sliding from "two functional poles" to "two lexical items in English" is the contested conclusion, and the only thing that licenses that slide is *English-internal* diagnostics that independently force two items. The section names those diagnostics (line 101) but never displays them, and it never engages the leading *one-item* analysis (Kadmon & Landman 1993, "Any," who derive both uses from a single indefinite via widening + strengthening; **needs source verification**, cited from memory). **Action:** display 2-3 English-internal diagnostics and actually test the unification defeater the paper already lists (line 105), citing the monist analysis. Half a paragraph of real "any" data (source-grounded, not generated) converts the section from slot-filling to a worked case.

- **The three-way distinction the prompt and abstract promise (comparison / classification / evidential transfer) is only two-way in the text.** Comparison and transfer are separated (line 87); classification, assigning a language-particular phenomenon to a comparandum, is folded into "comparison" via the comparanda/realization passage (line 85) but never given its own epistemic status. That matters because classification is exactly where importation sneaks in: classifying English "any" under a two-poled comparandum is the risky middle step. **Action:** define the triad explicitly, and annotate the worked case to show it does comparison and transfer *without* illegitimate classification (the split is re-derived from English-internal facts, not read off the comparandum's two poles).

- **The realism/instrumentalism tension with Haspelmath is unresolved, and it exposes a "your profiles are UG-lite" rejoinder (line 81 vs the abstract's "pluralist realism"; line 91).** Haspelmath treats comparative concepts as stipulated tools that are not instantiated in any grammar; the paper wants typological comparanda "real enough for inquiry." Those are in tension and the draft does not say how it resolves them. Relatedly, when a profile recurs crosslinguistically (the NPI/FC contrast "across environments," line 103), a Reiss-sympathetic reader will say you have smuggled in a universal and just declined to call it UG. **Action:** one paragraph distinguishing a defeasible functional-typological recurrence from a genetically specified UG primitive, and stating in what sense a comparandum is "real" (constructed tool, or worldly pattern) so the pluralist realism in the abstract is earned rather than asserted.

## 4. Defensible non-UG bridge, or only a vocabulary for auditing?

Both, unevenly. The paper decisively refutes the *monopoly* claim and supplies two things on the positive side: a schema that makes substantive, gradeable demands (you must specify an ordering relation and stabilizers, not merely diagnostics), and an existence argument that non-UG bridges already work (Haspelmath, Croft). That is more than a vocabulary. But the one place it tries to *run* a bridge to a verdict, the "any" case, fills the slots without executing them: a reader cannot tell from Sec 6 whether English "any" comes out one item or two, only that the framework could narrate either outcome. So the honest characterization is an **auditing schema plus a credible existence proof, one execution short of a fully worked bridge.** The negative thesis is carried; the positive thesis is demonstrated in form but not in force.

## 5. What would make this polished rather than merely promising

- **Carry one bridge end to end.** Pick the "any" case, show the English-internal diagnostics, say which way they point, and test at least one named defeater (the unified semantics; Kadmon & Landman, Chierchia 2013, Giannakidou, all **needing source verification**). This is the highest-leverage change: it is the difference between "the schema has slots" and "the schema adjudicates."
- **Consider elevating a typological caution the draft currently buries as a defeater (line 105):** crosslinguistic *lexicalization* differences are cheap and language-particular, so translation splits are weak bridges in general. Stated up front, this actually strengthens the anti-Reiss point: the French cue is near-inert on its own, which means whatever warrants splitting "any" is English-internal, so UG licenses nothing here. You can get a stronger conclusion than "relocate the warrant"; you can *deflate* the French evidence.
- **Connect your two in-house toolkits.** Sec 5 introduces comparandum / realization / cross-level coupling (line 85); Sec 6 then drops that vocabulary and uses only profile-talk. Map the worked case onto both (comparandum = the NPI/FC functional contrast; realizations = English "any," French *rien* / *n'importe quoi*; cross-level coupling = the bridge hypothesis) so the apparatus is exercised, not just asserted.
- Resolve the Haspelmath realism tension and add the triad (per Sec 3 above).

## 6. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The negative thesis is sound and the projectibility schema is a genuine, usable contribution, but the single worked case (the part meant to show a non-UG bridge actually bearing evidential weight without importing categories) is under-executed and ignores the standard one-item analysis of "any," so it currently exhibits the framework rather than defending a bridge. That is one focused revision pass, not a structural rebuild, hence R&R rather than reject.
