**1. Summary**

The paper argues that Reiss is right that crosslinguistic evidence needs a warrant, but wrong that Chomskyan UG is the exclusive warrant for linguistic evidential transfer.

**2. Strengths**

- The paper understands Reiss’s strongest point: linguistic evidence is theory-mediated, and crosslinguistic comparison is not automatically licensed by shared everyday labels like “English” and “French.”
- The projectibility framework is a useful audit device: target, profile, diagnostics, scope, and defeaters are exactly the right questions to ask of evidential transfer.
- The French `any` section is the sharpest local critique because it tests Reiss’s own example and correctly notes that French can cue an English distinction without by itself settling the English I-language analysis.

**3. Weaknesses**

- The “monopoly claim” is partly in Reiss’s text, but the paper overextends it. Reiss does write exclusivist sentences: in sec. 5.3.3 he answers the licensing question with “Only the belief in Universal Grammar,” and in the conclusion says UG-deniers have “no grounds for importing analytic categories.” So the paper is not inventing the target. But the paper repeatedly broadens that target into claims about “all legitimate linguistic targets,” “field-wide evidential rights,” and the “condition of possibility for linguistic comparison.” Reiss can disown that broader version because he explicitly stipulates that his `linguists` are Chomskyans and that his object is I-language. Action: make the target the written I-language-internal exclusivity claim, not “all linguistics.”

- The main counterexamples do not yet hit Reiss’s best retreat. Historical reconstruction gives a non-UG warrant for genealogical inference among historically related public/attested forms. Public standards give a non-UG target for norm-governed practices. Neither directly answers Reiss’s Kyle/Hisako case: choosing between extensionally equivalent grammars for an individual English I-language by appeal to Japanese or French evidence mediated through S0/UG. If Reiss retreats to “I only meant theoretical inquiry into I-languages,” much of Sections 7-8 becomes pluralist background, not a refutation. Action: either give a non-UG bridge for I-language-internal crosslinguistic comparison, or concede that the paper defeats only Reiss’s broader wording.

- The paper underuses Reiss’s own UG-object/I-language distinction. Reiss explicitly says one could believe in mental grammars “without believing in UG-object”; that is a gift to this paper. But the Section 2 sentence about “successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry” risks sounding like I-language inquiry itself needs UG. That concession is not warranted by Reiss’s chapter. Action: distinguish mental grammar inquiry from nativist UG-object inquiry and press Reiss on why cross-I-language comparison requires UG-object rather than weaker shared cognitive, semantic, acquisition, or functional constraints.

Supporting citations outside Reiss, including Haspelmath, Croft, Fortson, Hoeksema, Stainton, Santana, Aikhenvald, and others, need verification for this review.

**4. Key Question**

If Reiss restates the claim as: “for choosing between competing analyses of an individual I-language, evidence from another language is relevant only through a theory of S0/UG,” what is your non-UG warrant for that exact inference?

**5. Verdict**

Reject: the paper has a promising framework, but its strongest objections currently defeat a broader monopoly than Reiss needs to defend.

**F1. Reiss’s Likely Retreat**

The paper is not robust enough to the retreat, “I meant only I-language inquiry, not all of linguistics.” If Reiss retreats to that local claim, Sections 7 and 8 lose much of their force: historical-comparative reconstruction and public standards show that non-UG linguistic projects exist, but they do not show that French or Japanese evidence can help choose among analyses of an English I-language without UG.

The objection does not fully dissolve, because Reiss’s written chapter really does say more than the retreat: he frames UG as necessary for general linguistics, cross-language relevance, and importing analytic categories. But to survive the retreat, the paper must fight on Reiss’s strongest terrain: individual mental grammars, extensionally equivalent grammars, and crosslinguistic evidence as indirect evidence about S0.

**F2. Over-Concession**

Yes, the paper risks over-conceding. The sentence itself does not strictly say that I-language inquiry requires UG, but a Reiss-friendly reader can hear it that way. That is unnecessary and strategically bad.

Reiss’s own chapter separates mental grammar from UG-object: belief in I-language does not entail belief in UG-object. The paper should exploit this. It should say: even if the target is an individual mental grammar, Reiss still owes an argument that only UG-object licenses cross-I-language comparison. Mental grammar realism alone does not give him nativist UG.

**F3. Written Claim or Psychology?**

The legitimate target is Reiss’s written exclusivity claim, not his psychology. There is enough text to target: the French-English section, the Japanese-English example, and the conclusion all make UG the licensing condition for crosslinguistic evidential travel.

But the paper’s framing sometimes invites an avoidable disowning. “Monopoly claim” is defensible as shorthand if immediately tied to Reiss’s exact wording. Phrases like “turns colleagues into claimants,” “password,” and “jurisdiction” shift attention from the written argument to tone and motive. A hostile Reiss defender will say: I stipulated my object, I argued about I-language science, and you attacked a sociological posture. Tighten the paper around the textual exclusivity claim and drop the psychologizing pressure points.
