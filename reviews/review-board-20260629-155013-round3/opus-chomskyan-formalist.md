# Review: *Bridge Principles without UG*

**Reviewer persona:** Chomskyan formal syntactician, sympathetic to Reiss and to UG; holds that substantive UG (locality, binding, islands, category features) is a contentful theory of transfer conditions with failure modes, not a bare postulate.

**Round:** 3 — 2026-06-29

---

## What the paper is doing, charitably stated (Rapoport step)

Before the knives. The paper does not deny that crosslinguistic evidence needs a warrant, does not deny realism, does not deny that armchair judgements are theory-mediated empirical data, and does not deny that Chomskyan work has produced fruitful comparisons. It concedes all of that explicitly (Sec. 2) and even concedes that substantive UG "can offer precise hypotheses about locality, binding, polarity, category features, movement" (Sec. 2). The disagreement is narrow and well-chosen: it targets one written sentence — Reiss's claim that "Anyone who denies UG has no grounds for importing analytic categories from one language to the study of another" (Reiss Sec. 6) — and the parallel "Only the belief in Universal Grammar allows us to construct this kind of argument" (Reiss Sec. 5.3.3). The paper's real contribution is a three-way separation that Reiss's chapter does run together at the surface: (i) human languages form a domain, (ii) evidence may sometimes travel between members, (iii) only UG licenses that travel. That separation is genuinely clarifying, and I learned something from it: it is a cleaner diagnosis than the usual "UG isn't necessary" reply, because it isolates the *exclusion* step as the one that needs an argument Reiss never supplies. The audit schema (target / diagnostic / scope conditions / defeaters, Sec. 4) is operational and, to the author's credit, applied to the paper's *own* warrants (historical, public-language) as severely as to UG. That is good-faith methodology.

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper grants Reiss that crosslinguistic evidence needs a warrant but denies his exclusivity claim, arguing that warrants are general "bridge principles" auditable by a projectibility test, of which substantive UG is one legitimate filling and not the only one — with historical-comparative reconstruction offered as a worked non-UG counterexample.

## 2. Strengths (specific)

1. **The three-role separation is a real advance, and it is fairly grounded.** Sec. 1 ("two faces": evidential and classificatory) and Sec. 3 (domain-formation / transfer / exclusion) correctly observe that Reiss's chapter asserts a domain-defining claim (Sec. 4 reasoning (a): "It is the postulate of UG that defines the domain of inquiry"), an evidence-licensing claim (EAD 1, EAD 3), and an exclusion claim (Conclusion), and that Reiss does *not* explicitly separate the three. The exclusion step is where the load sits, and the paper is right that Reiss never argues it independently of the domain claim.

2. **It audits Reiss's own showcase, not a soft target, and turns Reiss's own footnote against him.** Sec. 6 runs the projectibility audit on French→English *any* (Reiss's EAD 3) and identifies a genuine technical defeater: French *rien* is entangled with negative-concord / negative-quantifier behaviour, so using it to stand for "the polarity-dependent member of an English split" smuggles in the destination classification. Crucially this is *Reiss's own* admission — his fn. 15 concedes "negative concord and negative quantifiers, dialect variation in French including a mismatch between standard written French and spoken varieties." The paper quotes this complication accurately and promotes it from footnote to substantive defeater. That is charitable-then-sharp done correctly.

3. **The framework raises the burden on itself.** The cue / scoped-bridge / robust-bridge grading (Sec. 4) and the closing list of failure conditions ("Projectibility is defeasible too… The account would fail if non-UG warrants kept collapsing into cue-level prompts," Conclusion) make the alternative testable rather than merely asserted. The historical case (Sec. 7) is explicitly held to the same target/diagnostic/defeater discipline, including real scope limits (within a family or contact history) and real defeaters (borrowing, areal diffusion, chance). This is the right way to argue the positive half.

## 3. Weaknesses (specific and actionable)

**W1 — The one worked non-UG counterexample is in the wrong cell of the transfer matrix.** This is the central problem. Historical-comparative reconstruction (Sec. 7) is *vertical* transfer within a related family. But every one of Reiss's EADs is *horizontal* transfer between *unrelated* languages: French/Japanese → English (EAD 3), Japanese *mo/ka* → English (Sec. 5.3.3), wh-movement across Mandarin, Quechua, Hungarian, Urdu (EAD 8). The paper itself concedes that for horizontal transfer "common descent is unavailable by construction" (Sec. 7) and that "a successful horizontal non-UG warrant would need its own support profile, held-out projections, and defeaters… The stronger positive typological case is a further project rather than a hidden premise" (Sec. 7). So on the exact terrain Reiss chose, the paper supplies a *demand* (audit it) and a *demotion* (the French clue is only a cue) but, by its own admission, no positive rival *license*. A generativist reads this as a category-shift: the refutation defeats a peripheral claim (transfer-in-general) while deferring the central one (unrelated-language transfer). *Actionable:* either work through at least one horizontal non-UG warrant for the *any*-split (e.g., a Haspelmath-style comparative-concept profile plus a defended recurrence relation that survives held-out languages), or foreground the honest narrower thesis already latent in Sec. 6 — that even for the horizontal case the *bare postulate* licenses no specific transfer, so the work is done by first-order diagnostics whatever fills the schema. Do not let the vertical counterexample silently stand in for the horizontal one.

**W2 — The thin/thick dilemma under-describes substantive UG and should be re-aimed at the exclusion role.** As written (Sec. 3): "A thin UG postulate can't license the monopoly; a thick UG theory is one auditable warrant among others." The second horn slides from a true claim (a thick theory does not, by being thick, *exclude* rival warrants for other targets) to a deflationary one (thick UG is *merely* one warrant among others, listed in Sec. 4 alongside "functional recurrence, diachronic pathway, processing pressure, acquisition bottleneck"). That flattening is exactly where the strawman risk lives. Reiss is explicit that UG is *both* postulate *and* "an articulated theory… subjected to elaboration and revision" with "two interdependent aspects" (Sec. 3, quoting Chomsky's LSLT); the EADs *are* the content. The generativist holds that the substantive constraints (locality, c-command, islands, the no-language-treats-O-and-transitive-S-alike gap of EAD 6, the NPI/anaphor c-command parallels of EAD 10) are not a menu item co-equal with diachrony — they are a *unified* theory of the shared faculty that *also* predicts the same constraints across *unrelated* languages, which diachrony provably cannot do. *Actionable:* tighten the second horn so the dilemma bites the exclusion step only: "a thick UG theory licenses particular transfers but does not, by being thick, license the *exclusion* of non-UG warrants for *other* targets." Drop or qualify "one auditable warrant among others," which is the phrase a referee will quote to accuse you of deflating the thing you elsewhere (Sec. 2) admit is contentful.

**W3 — The load-bearing monopoly sentence is paraphrased, not quoted, at the point it is introduced; and the I-language/UG-object wedge Reiss hands you is left unused (see F2, F3).** The paper's whole case rests on the exclusivity claim being *written* and *unconditional*. Yet at its first and most load-bearing statement (Sec. 1: "It says that denying UG leaves one without grounds for importing analytic categories from one language into another. That's the monopoly claim at issue here") the sentence is paraphrased, with the section cite parked later in Sec. 3. Quote Reiss's Conclusion verbatim ("Anyone who denies UG has no grounds…") and EAD 3 verbatim ("Only the belief in Universal Grammar allows us…") at the moment you name the target. This costs nothing and bulletproofs you against the "you're attacking my intentions" reply (F3). Relatedly, the paper *concedes* a link between I-language inquiry and UG that Reiss's own Sec. 3 does not require (see F2) — a missed opportunity to drive the wedge rather than hand back ground.

## 4. Key question for Q&A

Your single worked non-UG warrant — historical reconstruction — is vertical transfer within a family, but every EAD Reiss offers is horizontal transfer between *unrelated* languages, where you concede common descent is "unavailable by construction." For that case you give a demotion-to-cue and defer the positive warrant to "a further project." On the terrain Reiss actually chose, name the thick non-UG theory that promotes the French *any* cue to a warrant for English. If you can't yet name one, in what sense have you refuted the monopoly *for unrelated languages*, rather than merely relocated it to a target (related languages) Reiss wasn't talking about?

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The three-way diagnosis and the self-applied audit framework are a genuine contribution and the engagement is source-grounded and charitable; but the central counterexample is in the wrong transfer cell (W1), the thin/thick dilemma under-credits substantive UG and should be re-aimed at the exclusion role (W2), and the paper concedes an I-language→UG link that Reiss's own UG-object/UG-theory/I-language distinction does not demand (F2). These are fixable without abandoning the thesis.

## FOCUS questions

### F1. Robust to Reiss's "I meant only I-language inquiry, not all of linguistics" retreat?

**Mostly yes, and for the right reason — but the paper should say so explicitly.** Reiss has built the retreat into the chapter: Sec. 2 stipulates that "'linguists' in this chapter will refer to… the views of Chomsky and those who broadly adopt his philosophical stances," and "Since I take the I-language approach for granted, Pullum and Scholz's various objections… are irrelevant to my goals here." So he can say: "I only ever claimed that *within* the program of characterizing I-languages, crosslinguistic transfer is UG-licensed; I never said historical or public-language inquiry is illegitimate, only that it isn't my object."

The objection does **not** dissolve, because the paper's core argument is conducted *inside* an I-language transfer case. Reiss's French→English *any* (EAD 3) and the Rim/Sarah/Kyle/Hisako setup (EAD 1) are explicitly I-language-to-I-language transfers ("there is no such entity as English… there are just a bunch of individual I-languages," Reiss fn. 18). The thin/thick dilemma and the demotion-to-cue verdict (Sec. 6) therefore bite *even if* Reiss restricts himself to I-language inquiry: for an I-language transfer, the *bare* postulate still licenses no specific inference, and once locality/diagnostics/defeaters do the work, "the bare UG postulate isn't doing the work" (Sec. 6). That argument is retreat-proof and the paper should flag it as such.

What the retreat *does* neutralize is Sec. 8 (public/distributed objects): if Reiss withdraws to "I-language is simply my object," then public-language inquiry becomes a *different, non-competing* enterprise rather than a *refutation*, and Sec. 8 stops being a counter to the monopoly and becomes an independent program. The paper half-anticipates this ("Calling only I-language 'language' doesn't make the other targets disappear," Sec. 8) but never war-games the narrowest charitable reading — "transfer between I-language grammars in service of UG-theory" — on which Reiss's claim becomes nearly *definitional* (objects defined by a shared inventory are of course comparable via that inventory). *Actionable:* add a short paragraph stating the dilemma the retreat forces: either Reiss owns the unconditional Conclusion sentence (quantifying over "anyone" and "one language… another" — refuted by the historical case, which *is* importing categories across languages without a UG warrant), or he retreats to the near-tautology (which concedes the paper's point that UG doesn't own the transfer demand for non-I-language targets). Pin that and the retreat closes.

### F2. Does the paper over-concede in the end-of-Sec.-2 sentence?

**Yes — and the over-concession is unwarranted by Reiss's own text.** The sentence: "successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry don't establish that UG is the only possible licence, nor that every legitimate linguistic target has to be an I-language target." The noun phrase "UG-shaped warrants *for I-language inquiry*" presupposes that I-language inquiry's warrants are UG-shaped — it reads naturally as "the UG-shaped warrants that I-language inquiry uses," and so quietly grants that I-language inquiry as such is UG-warranted.

That concession is **more generous to Reiss than Reiss is to himself.** Reiss's Sec. 3 explicitly separates I-language from UG-object: "One could, in principle, be a psychologist who believes that humans have mental grammars, without believing in UG-object… So belief in I-language does not entail belief in UG-object." On Reiss's own distinction, mental-grammar / I-language inquiry can proceed via "general learning mechanisms" *without* the nativist UG-object. The bearer of the transfer-licensing role in Reiss's argument is specifically **UG-object** (the shared innate inventory in the Rim/Sarah scenario), not I-language inquiry per se and not mental-grammar realism. So the paper should *exploit* this wedge, not concede across it: even granting I-language realism, the transfer license attaches to UG-object, and Reiss himself admits I-languages-without-UG-object are coherent — which already shows I-language inquiry as such is not the thing that carries the warrant.

*Caveat that keeps this fair:* for *crosslinguistic transfer between* I-languages (as opposed to single-language description), Reiss does claim UG-object is required — and that is precisely the monopoly the paper means to contest. So the paper must not appear to grant it in passing in Sec. 2 and then attack it in Sec. 3. *Actionable:* rewrite the sentence to lean on Reiss's own three-way term distinction — e.g., "Reiss himself grants that mental grammars need not rest on UG-object (Sec. 3); the transfer-licensing role he assigns to UG therefore attaches to UG-*object* specifically, not to I-language inquiry as such — so even the I-language target does not, on Reiss's own terms, require UG to be studied." As written, the sentence hands the generativist a foothold (I-language inquiry is UG-shaped) that Reiss's text does not demand.

### F3. Is the target the *written* exclusivity claim, or Reiss's intentions/psychology?

**Officially the written claim, and the paper mostly stays disciplined — but there are two soft spots where Reiss can disown the attributed position.**

The good: the paper repeatedly anchors to text — "Reiss's conclusion," "the chapter," "Reiss's monopoly claim comes from a chain of roles assigned to UG, not from one local observation" (Sec. 3), with the cite \citep[secs.~1, 5--6]{reiss2026armchair}. It also pre-empts the cheap shot by flagging the "self-identify as linguists" phrasing as "not the argument" (Sec. 5). That restraint is correct.

The soft spots:

(a) **The "runs together / lets the first do the work of all three" framing risks attacking a position Reiss can disown.** The abstract ("three questions Reiss tends to run together") and Sec. 3 ("Reiss's chapter often lets the first do the work of all three") characterize Reiss's *reasoning habits*. But Reiss's Sec. 3 is itself an extended exercise in *distinguishing* senses — UG-object vs. UG-theory vs. I-language — and he would reply, with textual warrant, "I do not run my terms together; distinguishing them is the whole point of Section 3." The charitable and accurate move is to concede that Reiss carefully separates UG-object/UG-theory/I-language, and to locate the conflation *precisely* at the level he does *not* explicitly separate: domain-formation vs. transfer vs. exclusion. As written, "runs together" overreaches into a claim about all his distinctions and lets him disown it.

(b) **The central target is paraphrased, not quoted, where it is introduced** (see W3). Until the unconditional Conclusion sentence and the EAD 3 "only the belief in UG" sentence are quoted verbatim at the point of attack, a hostile Reiss can say the paper is arguing against an inflated reading of his "postulate" talk rather than his words. Both sentences exist and are unconditional; quote them and the psychology charge has nowhere to land.

Net: the paper's target *is* the written claim, and the written claim *is* genuinely exclusivity-shaped (not a strawman) — but two framing choices ("runs together"; paraphrase-not-quote) leave Reiss a disownable reading. Both are cheap to fix.
