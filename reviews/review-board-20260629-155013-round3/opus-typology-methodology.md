# Review: *Bridge Principles without UG*

**Reviewer stance:** typology / methodology in the Haspelmath–Croft tradition (comparative concepts vs. descriptive categories; Radical Construction Grammar). Primary concern: does the paper use comparative concepts as a *non-UG* discipline correctly, and does it keep **comparison**, **classification**, and **evidential transfer** distinct? Secondary: target-heterogeneity — does it tell genuine typological projection apart from calque, borrowing, and translation artefact?

**Sources read for this review:** the paper (`main.tex`) and Reiss's chapter (`reiss2025research.md`, manuscript dated 29 Aug 2025; cited in the paper as `reiss2026armchair`, forthcoming in the *Oxford Handbook of Philosophy of Linguistics*). Reiss attributions below are quoted from that manuscript. **All other cited sources (Haspelmath 2010, Croft 2001, Hoeksema 2012, Ladusaw 1979, Chierchia 2013, Stainton 2011, Santana 2016, Aikhenvald 2004, Göksel & Kerslake 2005, Fortson 2004, Pullum 2001, Milroy 1999, Agha 2005, and the author's own `reynolds20xx` entries) I have *not* read this session; claims attributed to them are flagged as needing verification.** I did confirm the bib entries for Haspelmath 2010 (*Language* 86(3): 663–687) and Croft 2001 (OUP) are bibliographically accurate.

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

Crosslinguistic evidence requires an auditable *bridge principle* (a declared projection target, support profile, diagnostics, scope, and defeaters), not specifically Chomskyan Universal Grammar; Reiss's monopoly claim conflates domain-formation, evidential transfer, and exclusivity, and his own French–English *any* case, audited, demotes the crosslinguistic data to a heuristic cue while the licensing runs language-internally.

## 2. Strengths (specific)

1. **The thin/thick dilemma is a genuinely good argument and it is Reiss-proof.** The paper's pivot (§3) — "A thin UG postulate can't license the monopoly; a thick UG theory is one auditable warrant among others" — is the load-bearing move and it is hard to escape. It turns Reiss's own Newton framing against him: physics transfer principles "are answerable to scope, measures, counterfactual expectations, and breakdown conditions," whereas Reiss's postulate is asked to "do the grouping work, the transfer work, and the exclusionary work at once." This correctly separates a *domain postulate* from a *transfer principle*, which Reiss does run together. Reiss writes that "it is the postulate of UG that defines the domain of inquiry" (sec. 4) and, separately, that the postulate "licenses us to treat empirical data from each language as potentially bearing on the analysis of all other languages" (sec. 6); the paper is right that these are not the same claim and that the second does not follow from the first.

2. **The "imported classification" critique of the French case is the best typological point in the paper, and it is correct.** At line 129 the paper observes that French *rien* "is entangled with negative-quantifier and negative concord item behaviour, not simply with the behaviour of English NPI *any*," so that making *rien* stand for the polarity member of an English split means "a classification has already been imported … the very classificatory shortcut Reiss says his opponents aren't entitled to make." This is exactly the comparative-concepts vs. descriptive-categories discipline applied with teeth: you cannot read an English descriptive category off a French descriptive category, and pretending you can smuggles the conclusion into the premise. It is also fair to Reiss, who himself flags in his footnote 15 "the existence of phenomena such as negative concord and negative quantifiers, dialect variation in French including a mismatch between standard written French and spoken varieties." The paper credits him with that awareness (line 135) and then shows the complications are *defeaters*, not footnotes.

3. **Target-heterogeneity is handled with discipline.** The paper does not let "crosslinguistic evidence" be one undifferentiated thing. The *avoir … ans* case (line 121) explicitly forces a choice of target — "age predication, duration expression, calque, bilingual transfer, or translation practice" — before French can bear on English; the Turkish evidentiality reductio (line 123) separates "Turkish grammaticalizes a contrast English may express lexically, pragmatically, or not at all" from "English *left* is secretly two past forms"; and translation into French is admitted only "as a diagnostic cue … not because it transfers a French category into English" (line 119). This is precisely the calque/borrowing/translation-artefact screening a typologist wants, and it is rare to see it done so cleanly.

## 3. Weaknesses (specific and actionable)

1. **Haspelmath and Croft are credited but not mobilized; they are closer to a name-drop than a working alliance — and they are the paper's strongest available allies.** They appear once (line 99), with accurate but thin two-sentence glosses, and are then dropped exactly where they would do the most work. Two missed opportunities: (a) In the French audit the paper *re-invents* the comparison/classification/transfer trichotomy under its own `reynolds2025comparanda` apparatus, when Haspelmath (2010) already supplies the canonical comparative-concept / descriptive-category distinction whose whole point is that the typological tradition *independently reached the paper's "demote to cue" verdict*: comparative concepts license comparison and nothing more, never the import of one language's category into another. Foregrounding that would show the demotion is not an ad hoc projectibility manoeuvre but settled typological methodology. (b) Croft's denial of universal syntactic categories is a *frontal* challenge to Reiss's EAD 2 ("recurrent categories") and EAD 7 ("UG as a set of building blocks"), where Reiss asserts that NPIs, wh-words, and the like "are real, natural objects" instantiated universally. The paper leaves that confrontation on the table. **Action:** in §4, replace one paragraph of in-house restatement with a worked use of Haspelmath's distinction on the *any* case, and add one sentence noting that Croft's position contradicts Reiss's universal-category claim directly, so that the non-UG discipline is shown operating, not merely cited. *(Haspelmath 2010 and Croft 2001 characterizations here rest on my background knowledge; verify against the sources before relying on them.)*

2. **The only *worked* non-UG crosslinguistic bridge is genealogical (related languages); the horizontal/typological bridge — Reiss's actual battlefield — is promised, not delivered.** Reiss's motivating cases are horizontal and cross-family: "evidence from Japanese can … bear on … the I-language attained by a speaker of English" (his sec. 5.3.1, quoting Chomsky 1986), and the Japanese *mo/ka* case where "without UG, the correlation would be a bizarre coincidence" (sec. 5.3.3). The paper's successful counterexample (§7) is historical-comparative, i.e. *common descent*, which by construction does not reach unrelated-language transfer — the paper concedes this at line 155 ("a successful horizontal non-UG warrant would need its own support profile … The stronger positive typological case is a further project"). The negative thesis (UG has no monopoly) goes through on the genealogical case alone, so the paper is not *wrong*. But a typology venue will read the title "Crosslinguistic Evidence" as promising the horizontal case and find an IOU. **Action:** work *one* horizontal typological bridge using the apparatus already gestured at — an implicational universal or semantic map (Haspelmath), where observing feature X in language L defeasibly licenses a projection about Y with cross-linguistic functional/statistical recurrence as the support profile and known exceptions as declared defeaters. That would (i) make the typological material load-bearing, (ii) answer *mo/ka* on its own ground, and (iii) close the gap currently labelled "a further project." Without it, consider reframing the title/abstract so the genealogical case is the advertised counterexample and horizontal typological transfer is explicitly marked unfinished.

3. **The historical-comparison counterexample under-answers the obvious Reissian retreat: that the comparative method presupposes UG-supplied phonological primitives.** Reiss holds (EAD 4, EAD 9, and Chomsky & Halle 1965 as he quotes them) that a universal feature inventory "cannot be avoided" and is part of UG. A Reissian will say: stating Latin *p* ~ Gothic *f* presupposes that the daughters share commensurable segmental primitives, and *those* are UG-object; so the comparative method covertly relies on UG. The paper's reply (line 151 — constraints "help shape the space of possible changes, but they don't license evidential transfer by themselves") is too quick. **Action:** deploy the rebuttal the paper already has the materials for. Correspondences are stated over *phonetic substance* and language-particular segments aligned by the linguist's comparative (etic) framework plus causal-historical continuity; and Reiss's own program (Reiss & Volenec) defends *substance-free* features, which therefore cannot be what grounds a phonetically defined correspondence set. So Reiss specifically cannot claim the comparative method needs *his* features. This is a clean, fair, and decisive move; make it explicitly. *(The substance-free-features attribution to Reiss & Volenec is consistent with Reiss's own text — he calls Reiss & Volenec 2022 "the sole contribution … that adopts and defends the nativist position" and the title references "substance free" — but verify the Reiss & Volenec characterization before leaning on it.)*

*Minor:* §8 (public/distributed objects, Standard English, the Armstrong-on-the-moon reply) is well done but answers the *ontological* half of Reiss's restriction, not the crosslinguistic-evidence thesis; the paper says as much (line 169). It reads as a second paper's worth of material and dilutes the typological core. Either compress it or frame the paper as explicitly two-pronged from the abstract onward. The intro's "evidential face / classificatory face" split (line 39) partly licenses it, but the social-ontology content overshoots classification.

## 4. Key question for Q&A

On your own reconstruction, the licensing of the English two-*any* analysis is done by English-internal diagnostics (downward-entailing environments, free-choice/modal contexts, Hoeksema's distributional classes), and the French split is "only a cue." But if the crosslinguistic data does no licensing work in the *any* case, then that case is not an instance of crosslinguistic evidential transfer *for anyone* — Reiss can simply agree that French alone doesn't settle it and that the full warrant is English-internal plus theory. So what, in your paper, is the *worked horizontal* example where unrelated-language evidence genuinely carries a projection that language-internal evidence does not already carry — and is the support profile there functional-typological recurrence (Haspelmath-style) rather than UG?

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The central argument (domain ≠ transfer ≠ monopoly; thin/thick dilemma; genealogical counterexample) is sound and defeats the written exclusivity claim, but the typological apparatus that gives the paper its identity for this venue is cited rather than worked, and the horizontal non-UG bridge is promised rather than delivered.

## FOCUS QUESTIONS

### F1. Is the paper robust to Reiss's likely retreat — "I meant only I-language inquiry, not all of linguistics"? Does the typological/comparative-concepts material give a non-UG target that survives the retreat?

**Robust, but not primarily because of the comparative-concepts material.** Two defenses carry the weight, and neither is the typological apparatus.

(a) *Textual.* Reiss's written conclusion is unrestricted. He writes: "Anyone who denies UG has no grounds for importing analytic categories from one language to the study of another. In this sense, the Chomskyan, UG-based perspective is explicitly more richly empirical than **competing theoretical approaches**" (sec. 6). "Anyone" is not restricted to his stipulated 'linguists', and "competing theoretical approaches" explicitly ranges over non-Chomskyans — so the conclusion, as written, claims authority over the whole field, not just over I-language inquiry. The retreat contradicts the text. **The paper should quote this "competing theoretical approaches" clause verbatim — it is the single strongest closer against the I-language retreat and the paper currently does not cite it.** The paper's reading (lines 37, 109) is otherwise correct and defensible.

(b) *A non-UG target that is not an I-language.* Reconstructed proto-forms are not I-languages, and the warrant for transfer among related daughters is causal-historical, not UG-theoretic. So even granting the retreat ("UG monopolizes *I-language* crosslinguistic inference"), the paper exhibits a legitimate crosslinguistic inference over a non-I-language target. The clever tu quoque at line 143 — Reiss's own framework treats the Human Language Faculty "as an evolved, inherited human trait," so descent-based comparative inference is already licit in his ontology — survives the retreat because it does not depend on the target being an I-language.

**Where the typological/comparative-concepts material stands:** it survives the retreat *as a discipline for comparison and classification* (Haspelmath built it explicitly as non-UG and non-aprioristic, so Reiss cannot annex it), but it does **not**, in this draft, supply a *worked* non-UG evidential-transfer target. The paper concedes the positive typological case is "a further project" (line 155). So the honest answer is: the paper is robust to the retreat, but the survivor is (i) the textual point and (ii) historical comparison — the comparative-concepts material is a supporting citation, not the load-bearing target. To make the typological material itself retreat-proof, deliver Weakness-2's worked horizontal bridge.

### F2. Does the paper over-concede at the §2 sentence about "successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry"?

**Yes, mildly, and the over-concession is in tension with the paper's own later argument — but it is not fatal.** The sentence (line 53) reads: "successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry don't establish that UG is the only possible licence." Read charitably it means "UG warrants, where they succeed, don't establish monopoly," which is fine and dialectically generous (Rapoport-appropriate). Read literally it concedes a general *track record* of success for the universal-category programme — and from the Haspelmath/Croft standpoint this reviewer is asked to occupy, that is a substantive give-away, since Croft's Radical Construction Grammar denies that there are universal syntactic categories at all, and Haspelmath denies that crosslinguistic categories are descriptive realities. More damaging internally: the concession sits awkwardly beside the paper's own thin/thick verdict that "a thick UG theory is one auditable warrant among others" (line 67). If a thick UG theory must declare target, support, scope, and defeaters and survive them like everything else, then whether it is "successful" is *exactly what is under audit* — the paper should not pre-grant it. **Action (cheap):** qualify to "UG-shaped warrants, where they succeed for I-language inquiry by declaring their targets and surviving their defeaters, still don't establish monopoly." That keeps the generosity, removes the unearned concession that the universal-category programme is methodologically vindicated, and aligns §2 with §3. The monopoly argument does not depend on the concession either way (success ≠ exclusivity), so this is a strategic tidy-up, not a rescue.

### F3. Is the central argumentative target the chapter's WRITTEN exclusivity claim or Reiss's intentions? Does the paper adequately credit Haspelmath/Croft, or just name-drop?

**Target: the written claim — correctly and disciplinedly.** The paper attributes claims to "the chapter," "Reiss's paper," and quoted text, not to Reiss's private intentions. At the one point where it touches rhetoric — the "self-identify as linguists" phrasing (sec. 2) "risks turning colleagues into claimants" (line 107) — it immediately fences it off: "That phrasing isn't the argument, but it marks the work the argument still owes." That is the right discipline. The reading that the written conclusion *exceeds* Reiss's I-language stipulation is defensible and, given the "competing theoretical approaches" clause, probably the most natural reading. (One caution for the author: Reiss does stipulate that "'linguists' in this chapter will refer to … Chomsky and those who broadly adopt his philosophical stances" (sec. 2) and that "I take the I-language approach for granted" (sec. 2); a hostile reader will try to scope the conclusion by the stipulation. The defense is precisely the unrestricted wording of the conclusion plus "competing theoretical approaches" — so quote it, per F1.)

**Crediting Haspelmath/Croft: real but thin — closer to a name-drop than a worked engagement.** The glosses are accurate (comparative concepts make comparison accountable without innate universals; RCG keeps categories language-specific while comparing via function and typological patterning), and the comparison/classification/transfer trichotomy is genuinely Haspelmathian in spirit. But the figures who *supply the paper's non-UG discipline* are cited once and then not used at the two places they would most help: the French audit (where Haspelmath's distinction independently yields the demotion verdict) and the historical-comparison commensurability problem (where Haspelmath's etic/comparative framework, not UG features, grounds the correspondences). Croft's frontal denial of universal categories — the direct contradiction of Reiss's EAD 2 / EAD 7 — is never joined. See Weakness 1 for the fix. As it stands, a typology/methodology venue will judge the engagement underdeveloped relative to its centrality to the paper's identity.

## Does the French *any* case exhibit a real non-UG crosslinguistic bridge?

**No — and the paper says so.** On the paper's own reconstruction the French data is "demoted … to a cue until the support profile and failure conditions are supplied" (line 137) and "isn't a robust bridge on Reiss's example alone" (line 133); the licensing is done by English-internal diagnostics (downward-entailing environments, free-choice/modal contexts, Hoeksema's distributional classes) plus a defended recurrence profile. So the *any* case is not a worked non-UG crosslinguistic bridge; it is a *demotion* of Reiss's example that relocates the real warrant language-internally. This is good Haspelmathian hygiene (you cannot read English classification off French), and it is honest, but it means the paper's only *delivered* non-UG crosslinguistic bridge is the genealogical one in §7 (related languages, causal-historical warrant). The dialectical risk the author should note: because the demotion shows the French data licenses nothing *for either side* without English-internal evidence, Reiss can absorb it ("I said French *bears on* English, not that it settles English single-handedly"). The demotion therefore wounds Reiss's *only-UG-licenses-this* phrasing but does not by itself produce the rival non-UG bridge the title advertises — that job is left to historical comparison and to the unwritten horizontal typological case.
