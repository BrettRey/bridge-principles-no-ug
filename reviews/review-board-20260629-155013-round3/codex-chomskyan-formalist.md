**1. Summary**

The paper argues that Reiss is right that crosslinguistic evidence needs a theory-mediated warrant, but wrong that Chomskyan UG is the unique warrant; projectibility, with explicit targets, diagnostics, scope conditions, and defeaters, can supply bridge principles without UG monopoly.

**2. Strengths**

1. The paper is charitable in the right place. Section 2 grants Reiss’s strongest point: armchair linguistics is empirical, categories are theory-mediated, and crosslinguistic evidence does not travel for free. That makes the reply much stronger than a familiar anti-UG dismissal.

2. The target/diagnostic/scope/defeater framework is useful. Section 4 gives a real audit procedure rather than merely saying “pluralism.” The distinction between cue, scoped bridge, and robust bridge is especially promising.

3. The French any discussion in Section 6 is the paper’s best local engagement. It correctly asks whether French evidence is a warrant for the English analysis or only a prompt to look for English-internal diagnostics.

**3. Weaknesses**

1. The thin/thick dilemma is too quick and risks missing actual generative practice. Reiss does not only offer a contentless password called UG. He explicitly presents UG as both postulate and theory, and his examples appeal to substantive machinery: NPIs, DEOs, c-command, locality, traces, features, reduplication, unaccusativity. A working generativist will say: the thin postulate licenses crosslinguistic relevance at the level of I-language inquiry; thick UG-theory supplies the local diagnostics and failure modes. The paper should revise Section 3 so the dilemma targets Reiss’s strongest position: not “bare UG licenses transfer,” but “UG-object plus developing UG-theory is the only framework in which such transfers are principled.”

2. The historical-comparative counterexample in Section 7 only partially hits Reiss’s target. It shows that non-UG bridge principles exist for diachronic reconstruction, but Reiss can reply that this concerns a different object: causal history of forms, not the choice between grammars for an individual’s I-language. Since Reiss says UG “allows data from each language to bear” on other language analyses, the paper needs at least one robust non-UG bridge for synchronic, mental-grammar inquiry, or it should narrow its claim to “UG is not the only bridge principle in linguistics broadly.”

3. Some rhetoric makes the opponent too easy to disown. “Monopoly claim” is defensible because Reiss’s conclusion says those who deny UG have “no grounds” for importing categories. But phrases like “turns colleagues into claimants” shift from the written argument to posture or psychology. For a venue review, I would advise cutting or cooling that language and saying instead: Reiss’s local stipulation about ‘linguists’ becomes unstable when the chapter later makes field-wide claims.

**4. Key Q&A Question**

Can you give a genuinely synchronic, I-language-compatible, non-UG bridge principle that licenses evidence from Japanese or French for the grammar of an English speaker, rather than a diachronic bridge for historical reconstruction or a public-language bridge for standards and norms?

**5. Verdict**

Revise & Resubmit: the core projectibility framework is promising, but the paper must engage Reiss’s thick, contentful UG practice rather than mainly defeating a bare-postulate version.

**F1. Reiss’s likely retreat to I-language inquiry**

If Reiss retreats to “I meant only I-language inquiry, not all linguistics,” the paper still has a target, but a narrower one than the current framing suggests. Reiss’s text gives the paper a legitimate target because he writes broadly: UG licenses cross-language relevance, and in the conclusion those denying UG lack “no grounds” for importing categories. That is an exclusivity claim in the written chapter.

But the retreat would weaken Sections 7 and 8. Historical reconstruction and public standards show that non-UG linguistic inquiry can be principled, but Reiss can say: those are not the objects I was defending. The paper’s strongest remaining target would then be Section 6: whether Reiss’s French-English any argument really requires UG rather than English-internal diagnostics plus a semantic/distributional profile. To survive the retreat, the paper should foreground that local I-language case and add a non-UG internalist bridge.

**F2. Does the paper over-concede?**

Yes, it risks over-conceding. The sentence in Section 2 does not strictly say I-language inquiry requires UG, but it may be read that way because it grants “successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry” and then pivots to non-I-language targets.

That concession is not warranted by Reiss’s own distinctions. In Section 3, Reiss explicitly says “belief in I-language does not entail belief in UG-object.” That is a major opening the paper underuses. One can accept mental grammars as individual, internal, intensional objects while denying a language-specific innate inventory. The paper should press this harder: even for I-language inquiry, UG-object is not conceptually forced by mental grammar alone. Reiss’s stronger claim is empirical-methodological, not definitional.

**F3. Written monopoly claim or Reiss’s intentions?**

The paper should make the written exclusivity claim its target, not Reiss’s intentions. There is enough textual basis: Reiss says UG is needed to define the domain, to make French/Japanese evidence relevant to English, and that denial of UG leaves one with “no grounds” for category transfer. That is the position to attack.

But the current framing sometimes invites an easy disavowal. Reiss can say: I stipulated ‘linguists’ for this chapter; I did not mean all scholars of language; I was defending Chomskyan armchair inquiry against empiricist misunderstanding. To avoid that escape, the paper should say: even granting the stipulation, the chapter’s conclusion and examples overrun it. The issue is not Reiss’s psychology; it is the written slide from local Chomskyan methodology to exclusive licensing claims.
