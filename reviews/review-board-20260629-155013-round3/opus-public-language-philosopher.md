# Review: *Bridge Principles without UG*

**Reviewer persona:** Philosopher of language sympathetic to public-language and distributed-language ontologies (Stainton, Santana).
**Round:** 3
**Date:** 2026-06-29

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper grants Reiss's point that crosslinguistic evidence needs a warrant but denies his exclusivity claim, arguing that a projectibility audit (target, profile, diagnostics, defeaters) supplies auditable bridge principles, with historical-comparative reconstruction as a worked non-UG warrant and public/distributed standards as legitimate non-I-language targets disciplined by the same audit.

## 2. Strengths

1. **The thin/thick dilemma is the load-bearing move and it is well made.** The paper's central argument does not rest on rhetoric: "A thin UG postulate can't license the monopoly; a thick UG theory is one auditable warrant among others" (Sec. 3). This is the right pressure point. It is grounded in Reiss's own text, which oscillates between UG-as-postulate ("a status similar to an axiom in mathematics," Reiss Sec. 3) and UG-as-substantive-theory ("the existence of such innate categories," Sec. 5.2). The paper exploits exactly this ambiguity, which Reiss himself flags as the "systematic ambiguity" of UG-object vs. UG-theory but never resolves in favour of the monopoly.

2. **Source grounding on Reiss is genuinely careful.** Where the paper attributes a claim to Reiss it is accurate. The boundary-stipulation quote is verbatim ("Chomsky and those who broadly adopt his philosophical stances," Reiss Sec. 2). The "self-identify as linguists" reading is fair (Reiss Sec. 2 twice uses "self-identify"/"self-identified linguists"). The French *any* reconstruction matches Reiss's EAD 3 down to the data (`rien` / `n'importe quoi`), and the paper correctly credits Reiss with already noting the complications it then weaponizes ("negative concord ... dialect variation in French including a mismatch between standard written French and spoken varieties," Reiss fn. 15). This is the strongest part of the paper for a referee worried about strawmanning.

3. **The paper imposes its own discipline on its own objects.** Section 8 does not exempt public standards from the audit it demands of Reiss: "That demand is a strength of the alternative, because it imposes the same discipline on non-UG objects that Reiss rightly imposes on crosslinguistic inference" (Sec. 8). The defeater list for Standard English (Sec. 8) does real individuation work by saying where the object *stops* (edited prose, not casual speech; not all Englishes; not individual competence). That negative individuation is more than relabeling.

## 3. Weaknesses (the public-language section is where the paper is exposed)

1. **The positive individuation of public objects is under-committed, and as written it is open to the "relabeling" charge.** The paper says a projectibility reply "individuates public, community-level, and normatively regimented objects by the projections they support and the failure conditions they accept" (Sec. 8). But "the projections *it* supports" presupposes that "it" is already fixed. What positively unifies the in-scope features into one object is, on the paper's own telling, the maintenance machinery: "correction, publication, assessment, and uptake help stabilize the edited standard" (Sec. 8). A Reiss-sympathetic reader (and a Stainton-sympathetic one) will say: the self-maintaining norm is doing the individuating, and "projectibility" is the word for the fact that the norm is stable enough to predict from. The paper's own hedge concedes the gap: "Uptake, correction, transmission, and institutional maintenance may be stabilizing backgrounds, parts of the profile, or mere diagnostics depending on the projection target" (Sec. 8). If maintenance is a "mere diagnostic," the norm is the object and projectibility is only evidence; individuation has been *presupposed*, not *supplied*. **Actionable fix:** commit to the HPC reading explicitly. Say that the social-maintenance mechanism (schooling, editing, institutional enforcement) is the homeostatic cluster-stabilizer and is therefore *constitutive* of the kind, and that projectibility is the *individuation criterion* that picks out this cluster rather than mere evidence that it is there. That converts a gesture into an answer to the individuation worry. Leaving the constitutive/diagnostic question open (as Sec. 8 does) is precisely what invites the relabeling verdict.

2. **The moon rebuttal is sound against the test Reiss states but not against the criterion that is actually driving him.** Reiss's written line is a *location/concreteness* claim deployed for topic-scoping: "I-languages are concrete in the sense of having spatiotemporal locations inside the heads of individuals, whereas it is impossible to say anything useful about where an artificial construct like Standard English exists. In 1969 was it briefly on the moon with Neil Armstrong?" (Reiss Sec. 2). The paper's parody ("Armstrong didn't bring NASA command authority, the flight plan, radio protocol, or mission time to the lunar surface ... The transport test is the wrong test for distributed objects," Sec. 8) cleanly defeats a literal transport test. But it helps Reiss against the criterion he actually cares about: NASA command authority, flight plans, and mission protocol are paradigm *institutional/intentional* objects, exactly the class Chomsky brackets out of naturalistic inquiry. So the parallel concedes that public languages are institution-like and then leaves standing the Chomskyan reply: institutions are not natural objects and not the subject matter of a naturalistic science of the language faculty. The rebuttal also leans on an under-argued step: "his actions there were partly constituted by those distributed norms" (Sec. 8). Constitution of *actions* by norms does not entail that the norms are *located* anywhere, nor that they are objects of *linguistic* science; Reiss can grant action-constitution and repeat that the norm has no determinate location, which was his point. **Actionable fix:** separate the transport test (defeated) from the natural-object/concreteness criterion (not yet touched), and either defeat the latter or, more honestly, concede it and argue that non-naturalistic-but-still-scientific inquiry into distributed objects is legitimate. This is where Stainton, Santana, and Pullum must do real argumentative work rather than be name-checked (see source flags below).

3. **The public-language material is methodologically unified with the crosslinguistic argument but dialectically optional, and as written it reads as a second paper sharing a method.** The paper itself partitions the dispute into an "evidential half" and an "ontological half" (Sec. 8), and concedes that Section 8 is "rather than adding another case of crosslinguistic evidential transfer." The headline ("Bridge Principles without UG") and the decisive counterexample (historical comparison, Sec. 7) belong to the evidential half; the evidential monopoly is already refuted before Section 8 begins. The public-language ontology is therefore not *needed* by the paper's main thesis, which is the bolted-on smell. **Actionable fix:** there is a hinge the paper underuses. Typological comparanda (Haspelmath's comparative concepts, invoked in both Secs. 4 and 7) are themselves *public, abstracted, non-mental* objects; horizontal transfer between unrelated languages, which the historical warrant explicitly cannot cover (Sec. 7: "common descent is unavailable by construction"), requires precisely such public comparanda. Route the connection through that point: the objects that license horizontal crosslinguistic transfer are public/distributed objects, so the ontological half is a *precondition* of completing the evidential half, not a parallel application. That makes Section 8 load-bearing.

## 4. Key question for Q&A

You individuate Standard English by the projections it supports under declared defeaters, and you locate its stability in correction, schooling, and institutional maintenance. Is the maintenance mechanism *constitutive* of the object (so that projectibility is your individuation criterion and you have answered Reiss), or is it merely a *diagnostic* that the object is present (so that the maintained norm individuates and projectibility is only your inspection method)? Section 8 says it "may be" either; the whole question of whether you have answered the individuation worry or relabeled a self-maintaining norm turns on which answer you give, so which is it?

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The evidential argument (thin/thick dilemma plus the historical-comparison counterexample) is sound, well-grounded in Reiss, and on its own defeats the written exclusivity claim; but Section 8 under-commits on individuation, mis-locates Reiss's criterion in the moon rebuttal, and is not yet shown to be necessary to the paper's thesis, so the public/distributed-objects half needs the three fixes above before it carries its weight.

## FOCUS QUESTIONS

### F1. Is the paper robust to Reiss's likely retreat ("I meant only I-language inquiry, not all of linguistics"), and does the public-language ontology give a target that survives the retreat?

**Partly, and the robustness lives in the written conclusion, not the moon joke.** The retreat has real textual support: "Since I take the I-language approach for granted" (Reiss Sec. 2); the stipulation of "linguists" as Chomskyans (Reiss Sec. 2); and "under the I-language perspective, there is no such entity as English" (Reiss fn. 18), which is explicitly scoped to *that perspective*. If Reiss retreats here, much of Section 8's polemic is answering a claim he can say he never intended.

The paper's anchor against the retreat is the *unscoped* conclusion sentence: "Anyone who denies UG has no grounds for importing analytic categories from one language to the study of another" (Reiss Sec. 6). That sentence is not scoped to I-language inquiry; it quantifies over anyone, and the historical-comparison counterexample (Sec. 7) falsifies it directly, since comparative reconstruction imports categories across languages on a causal-genealogical licence with no UG warrant. So the *evidential* target survives the retreat cleanly.

For the public-language ontology specifically, the retreat actually *concedes* the paper's Section 8 thesis: if Reiss says "I only meant I-language inquiry; I never denied that public languages or sociolinguistic standards are legitimate objects," he has granted ontological pluralism, which is what Section 8 argues for. (Note that Reiss's own text already half-concedes this: he reports, without endorsing the rejection, that "Stainton (2011) finds the rejection by I-linguists of 'public languages' as potentially fruitful objects of study to be unfounded," Sec. 2.) So the public-language ontology gives a target that survives the retreat only in this deflationary sense: the retreat is a win for Section 8 but reframes the whole disagreement as scope/wording rather than substance. The paper does *not* currently stage this dilemma explicitly, and it should. **Recommended addition:** a short paragraph that (a) quotes the unscoped conclusion sentence, (b) hands Reiss the retreat, and (c) shows the retreat concedes both of the paper's theses (non-UG warrants exist; non-I-language targets are legitimate). That converts the retreat from an escape into a fork: hold the unscoped claim and be refuted by historical comparison, or retreat and concede pluralism. As written, the paper wins on the evidential target but leaves the public-language target resting on a charitable reading of a one-line joke (see F3).

### F2. Does the paper over-concede at the Section 2 sentence about "successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry"?

**Mildly, and it is a wording slip rather than a substantive one, but it is exploitable and should be tightened.** The sentence reads: "successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry don't establish that UG is the only possible licence, nor that every legitimate linguistic target has to be an I-language target" (Sec. 2). The phrase "successful UG-shaped warrants" grants that UG is doing licensing work. But the paper's own considered view (Sec. 3) is that the bare postulate does *no* licensing — "the warrant comes from the specified theory, diagnostics, and failure conditions, not from the bare fact that UG has been postulated" — and that a thick UG theory is merely "one auditable warrant among others." On that view there are no distinctively "UG-shaped" warrants; there are profile-and-diagnostic-shaped warrants, some of which happen to be filled by a substantive UG theory. The risk is concrete: Reiss can quote "successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry" back as the critic conceding that UG licenses I-language transfer, which is most of what he wants. **Fix:** rephrase so the success belongs to the thick, first-order theory plus its diagnostics and defeaters, not to UG-the-postulate, for example "warrants shaped by substantive UG-theoretic hypotheses succeed for I-language inquiry, and that very fact makes them first-order warrants under audit, not a monopoly licence." This keeps the (correct, Rapoport-honest) concession that Chomskyan work delivers real results while denying that the *postulate* is what licenses them.

### F3. Is the paper's central argumentative target the chapter's WRITTEN exclusivity claim, or Reiss's intentions?

**The load-bearing target is the written claim, and that is the right choice and well executed; but Section 8 drifts toward a reconstructed, stronger-than-written Reiss.** The evidential argument is pinned to text: the paper paraphrases and cites the unscoped conclusion ("denying UG leaves one without grounds for importing analytic categories from one language into another," Sec. 1, tracking Reiss Sec. 6) and builds the monopoly structure from Reiss's Secs. 1, 5--6. That is targeting the written exclusivity claim, not Reiss's psychology.

Section 8 is where the target slips. The paper says Reiss "presses an individuation worry against public languages" and speaks of "Reiss's ontological restriction" (Sec. 8). But Reiss's written remark is a *location/concreteness* claim ("impossible to say anything useful about where ... Standard English exists," Sec. 2) deployed to scope his topic ("irrelevant to my goals here"), not a developed individuation thesis and not a prohibition on others studying public languages. "The joke points to an individuation challenge" (Sec. 8) is the paper's own gloss, a charitable strengthening of a one-liner. So in Section 8 the paper is partly arguing against an intended/reconstructed Reiss rather than the written text. This is not a fatal flaw, but it should be made honest: either (a) quote the moon sentence verbatim and argue that, in context, it does exclusivity work (against Pullum-Scholz's model-theoretic alternative), or (b) concede that the moon line is topic-scoping and re-pin the public-language rebuttal to the unscoped conclusion sentence, which *is* written and *is* the legitimate target. Option (b) is cleaner and also fixes F1.

## Source-grounding flags (per house LAW; reviewer has read only Reiss and the paper under review)

I have read **Reiss (2025/2026)** and the paper. Every claim attributed to Reiss above is quoted from the chapter. The following citations in the paper I have **not** verified and flag for the author to source-check before resubmission:

- **`stainton2011public`** -- The paper characterizes Stainton as holding that "the object is understood as a humanly individuated system whose evidence base includes deployment, uptake, and psychological facts" (Sec. 8). The reference itself is confirmed by Reiss's bibliography (Stainton 2011, *In Defense of Public Languages*, *Linguistics and Philosophy* 34:479--488) and Reiss's gloss matches the title, but the specific paraphrase ("humanly individuated system," the listed evidence base) must be checked against Stainton's text. This matters: "humanly individuated" is doing a lot of work for the individuation answer, so the paraphrase needs to be exact.
- **`santana2016language`** -- "Santana's pluralist treatment of language" (Sec. 8). Not in Reiss's references; I have not read it. Verify existence, year, venue, and that "pluralist" is Santana's own framing rather than the paper's.
- **`pullum2001`** -- "Model-theoretic syntax makes room for formally characterizing public constructs without treating them as mental grammars" (Sec. 8). Consistent with Reiss's own report of Pullum & Scholz (2001b), but confirm the key resolves to the right Pullum & Scholz paper and year, and that the characterization is theirs.
- **`agha2005`, `milroy1999`** -- enregisterment and standardization "supply mechanisms for norm uptake and public recognizability" (Sec. 8). *Enregisterment* is Agha's term, so the attribution is plausible, but verify the year (Agha's enregisterment paper is commonly 2005, *J. Linguistic Anthropology*; there is also Agha 2003). Verify Milroy 1999 (James vs. Lesley Milroy) and the specific mechanism claim.
- **`haspelmath2010`, `croft2001`** -- comparative concepts and Radical Construction Grammar (Secs. 4, 7). Correctly characterized at a high level and Haspelmath is in the same handbook ("Haspelmath, this volume," Reiss Sec. 5.3.2), but page-level claims should be checked.
- **Linguistic data** -- the Grimm's Law correspondence set (Latin *pater*, Greek *patēr*, Sanskrit *pitar-*, Gothic *fadar*; Germanic *f* ~ non-Germanic *p*) and the Turkish indirect-evidential characterization are standard and look correct, but per source-grounding LAW they should carry page citations to `fortson2004`, `aikhenvald2004`/`GokselKerslake2005`. Lower priority for this venue, but flag.
- **`reynolds2026whatLanguage`, `reynolds2026varieties`, `reynolds2026kindsProjectibilityProfiles`, `reynolds2025comparanda`** -- author's own; not verified here.
