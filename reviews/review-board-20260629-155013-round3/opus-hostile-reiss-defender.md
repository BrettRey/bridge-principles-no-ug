# Hostile review (Reiss-defender): *Bridge Principles without UG*

Reviewer stance: I have just read Reiss's chapter, I think it is right, and I want
to reject this paper. I am bound by Rapoport's Rules and by source grounding: where I
attribute a claim to Reiss I quote the chapter; where the paper cites sources I have
not read, I flag them. I went in expecting to show that the "monopoly claim" is the
author's paraphrase rather than Reiss's text. That line failed on inspection, and I
say so below. My real objection is narrower and survives: the paper refutes the
*literal universal* form of Reiss's claim while leaving the *scoped* form (which is
the one Reiss cares about) standing, and it spends a third of its length attacking
positions Reiss explicitly brackets.

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

Crosslinguistic evidence needs an auditable bridge principle, not Universal Grammar:
projectibility (target / profile / diagnostic / scope / defeaters) supplies the audit,
historical-comparative reconstruction supplies a worked non-UG warrant, and so UG is
one possible licence for evidential travel rather than the only one.

## 2. Strengths (specific)

1. **The thin/thick dilemma (Sec. 3, p. 67) is a real argument, not a rhetorical
   move.** "If UG merely makes crosslinguistic evidence potentially relevant, the
   monopoly conclusion doesn't follow: potential relevance isn't a licence for any
   particular transfer. If, instead, a substantive UG theory licenses a particular
   transfer, then UG is no longer functioning as a bare domain-defining postulate."
   This squarely engages the chapter's central commitment that UG is "a postulate
   that allows empirical work to proceed" (Reiss §1) rather than a first-order theory.
   It is the paper's best contribution and the part I cannot wave away.

2. **The historical-comparative counterexample (Sec. 7) is well chosen and honestly
   bounded.** It defeats the *literal* wording of Reiss's conclusion. Reiss writes:
   "Anyone who denies UG has no grounds for importing analytic categories from one
   language to the study of another" (§6). The comparative method imports analytic
   categories (cognate sets, correspondences, reconstructed forms) from one language
   to another, and its licence is common descent, not UG. The paper does not overclaim
   here: it concedes the warrant is family-bounded and vertical (p. 153), and it does
   not pretend reconstruction settles the horizontal case.

3. **The projectibility audit is reusable and the French-*any* reconstruction (Sec. 6)
   is careful.** It demotes the French data to "cue" status rather than declaring
   Reiss refuted, and it correctly notes that Reiss himself flags negative concord,
   French dialect variation, and the written/spoken mismatch (the paper's attribution
   on p. 135 matches Reiss's footnote 15 exactly: "negative concord and negative
   quantifiers, dialect variation in French including a mismatch between standard
   written French and spoken varieties"). Source handling at the level of individual
   attributions is generally accurate.

## 3. Weaknesses (specific and actionable)

**W1 — The decisive scope question is never closed (the core defect; see F1).**
Reiss states the exclusivity *three times*, and each time it is tied to the *horizontal*
transfer of grammatical categories for I-language analysis:

- §5.3.1: "Without the postulate of UG, there is no reason to think that data from
  Japanese should be any more relevant to the choice of which English grammar is
  correct than data concerning bird migration or soap bubbles."
- §5.3.3: "What licenses this reasoning? Only the belief in Universal Grammar allows
  us to construct this kind of argument."
- §6: "Anyone who denies UG has no grounds for importing analytic categories from one
  language to the study of another."

The paper's *only fully worked* non-UG success is vertical (historical). For the
horizontal case that Reiss's argument actually lives in (Japanese-on-English), the
paper demotes French to a cue and concedes that "the stronger positive typological
case is a further project rather than a hidden premise" (p. 155). So a defender can
narrow Reiss's claim to "UG licenses horizontal transfer of grammatical categories
between unrelated languages for I-language analysis" and the paper has, by its own
admission, no worked counterexample. *Action:* either carry one horizontal,
unrelated-language non-UG warrant through to scoped-bridge status (a Haspelmath
comparative-concept case with declared profile, held-out projections, and defeaters),
or drop the positive horizontal pretension and state plainly that the thesis is the
negative one: UG is not the *sole* licence for *any* evidential travel. The conclusion
already half-admits this ("The claim here is the negative one Reiss denies," p. 155);
the rest of the paper should stop implying more.

**W2 — Section 8 attacks a position Reiss explicitly brackets (see F3).** Reiss does
not claim public languages are illegitimate objects; he says they are a *different
kind of object he is not studying*: "there is no point in comparing theories whose
objects are completely different kinds of things ... it is impossible to say anything
useful about where an artificial construct like Standard English exists" (§2), and
"Pullum and Scholz's various objections ... are irrelevant to my goals here" (§2). The
moon joke is the punchline of a *methodological self-limitation*, not an ontological
ban. When the paper writes that the transport test "would erase much of what made the
trip intelligible" (p. 165) and "Calling only I-language 'language' doesn't make the
other targets disappear" (p. 169), it is answering a claim Reiss can disown: he never
said they disappear. *Action:* quote §2, reframe Section 8 as "bracketing is not
refuting" rather than "Reiss restricts/erases public objects," and cut "turns
colleagues into claimants" (p. 107), which the paper itself concedes "isn't the
argument."

**W3 — The paper under-quotes Reiss at exactly the load-bearing moments, and never
engages §3.** The monopoly claim is *paraphrased* (p. 37) but never quoted, even
though the verbatim sentence is stronger evidence for the paper's reading than the
paraphrase. More seriously, the paper never engages Reiss's §3 UG-object / UG-theory
distinction, although the thin/thick dilemma is a close cousin of it. Reiss already
draws the postulate-vs-articulated-theory line ("General discussion of the existence
and content of UG, of course, also treats the idea as a hypothesis and part of an
articulated theory," §3) and already issues the challenge the paper means to answer:
"Anyone who wants to reject the postulate or hypothesis of UG will need to provide
alternative accounts of the results sketched below in section 5" (§3). The paper
should cite §3, credit the distinction, and meet that challenge for at least one EAD
rather than reinventing the distinction uncredited. *Action:* add a paragraph in
Sec. 3 quoting §3 and §6, and pick one EAD (NPI/DEO, or the *mo/ka* case in §5.3.3)
to show a non-UG account of "the results."

*Sources I have not read and therefore flag (source grounding):* haspelmath2010,
croft2001, stainton2011public, santana2016language, pullum2001, milroy1999, agha2005,
hoeksema2012, ladusaw1979, chierchia_logic_2013, aikhenvald2004, GokselKerslake2005,
fortson2004, huddleston2002, Goodman1955, and the reynolds20xx self-citations. The
Stainton use is at least internally corroborated: Reiss himself cites Stainton (2011)
as holding "the rejection by I-linguists of 'public languages' as potentially fruitful
objects of study to be unfounded" (§2), which matches the paper's use. The Grimm's-Law
cognate set (Latin *pater*, Greek *patēr*, Sanskrit *pitar-*, Gothic *fadar*; Germanic
*f* ~ non-Germanic *p*) is textbook-standard but I have not checked it against
fortson2004 here; the page-free citation is acceptable but should not be treated as
verified. Minor: the bib key is `reiss2026armchair` while the source is dated
2025 ("To appear"); confirm the forthcoming year.

## 4. Question I would ask at Q&A

Your only fully worked non-UG warrant is historical-comparative, which is
family-bounded and vertical. Reiss's exclusivity claims are paradigmatically about
*horizontal* transfer between unrelated languages ("data from Japanese ... the choice
of which English grammar is correct," §5.3.1). Can you exhibit a single worked
horizontal non-UG warrant that reaches scoped-bridge status? If not, doesn't your
thesis reduce to the negative claim "UG is not the *sole* licence," which Reiss
survives intact by restricting his exclusivity to horizontal grammatical-category
transfer for I-language analysis?

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The central target is textually real (the "you invented the
monopoly claim" objection fails), and the thin/thick dilemma plus the historical
counterexample land against the literal universal claim; but the scope retreat is not
foreclosed, Section 8 attacks a bracketed position, and the decisive passages are
under-quoted. All three are fixable without abandoning the thesis.

## FOCUS questions

### F1. Is the paper robust to Reiss's retreat, "I meant only I-language inquiry, not all of linguistics"?

**Partly robust, partly not — and I can make the retreat work for the half that
matters to me.**

First, the honest concession a fair defender must make: the retreat does **not**
rescue the *literal* wording of the conclusion. "Anyone who denies UG has no grounds
for importing analytic categories from one language to the study of another" (§6) is,
read as written, false, and the comparative method shows it. Neogrammarians imported
analytic categories across languages on the licence of common descent without any UG
commitment. The paper's Section 7 earns that point.

But the retreat **does** work where Reiss actually fights. His three exclusivity
statements are all about treating *each language's data as potentially bearing on
every other's* — the universal, horizontal base ("the postulate of UG expands the
empirical base for an analysis of English to include data from all human languages,"
§5.3.1). Common descent is exactly the wrong tool here: it licenses transfer *within*
a family, not from any language to any other, and certainly not Japanese-to-English.
The paper concedes this in two places (pp. 153, 155). So a defender narrows the claim
to: "UG is the licence for *horizontal* transfer of grammatical categories between
arbitrary (including unrelated) languages, in the analysis of I-languages." Against
that narrowed claim the paper offers no worked counterexample — only a demotion of the
French case to cue status and a promissory note about typology. The historical case,
which Reiss never discusses, is a *different kind of object* in exactly the sense Reiss
licenses himself to set aside (§2: "completely different kinds of things"). So Reiss
retreats to the local claim and keeps his prize: he can grant the comparative method,
grant public languages as a separate object, and still say that for the universal
horizontal base — the thing the chapter is about — the paper has shown only that the
French example is underdetermined, not that any non-UG warrant does the job. The
paper's target does not *dissolve* (the literal §6 sentence is genuinely over-broad and
genuinely refuted), but it *shrinks to the negative claim*, and the paper should admit
that is all it has positively established.

### F2. Does the paper over-concede near the end of Section 2?

The sentence: "successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry don't establish
that UG is the only possible licence, nor that every legitimate linguistic target has
to be an I-language target" (p. 53).

**It does not commit the fatal over-concession, but it forgoes a free win and its
packaging drifts the wrong way.** Read strictly, the sentence concedes only that UG
warrants are *successful for* I-language inquiry; it does not say I-language inquiry
*requires* UG. That distinction matters, because if the paper had conceded the
entailment "I-language inquiry requires UG," it would be handing Reiss *more than Reiss
grants himself*. Reiss is explicit: "One could, in principle, be a psychologist who
believes that humans have mental grammars, without believing in UG-object ... So
belief in I-language does not entail belief in UG-object" (§3). I-language is mental
grammar; UG-object is the innate faculty; the entailment runs UG-object → I-language,
not the reverse.

So the warranted reading of Reiss is that *crosslinguistic transfer* within I-language
inquiry needs UG, not that I-language inquiry as such needs UG. The paper's sentence is
*consistent* with that, which is why it is not a fatal slip. But the paper repeatedly
packages "UG" and "I-language" together — "UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry,"
"Chomskyan I-language inquiry can meet that demand for its own targets" (conclusion,
p. 191) — and thereby treats the I-language domain as UG's secure home turf. Reiss's
own UG-object/I-language distinction leaves room to deny even the *I-language-internal*
monopoly: a general-learning-mechanism mental-grammar theorist is doing I-language
inquiry without UG-object. The paper could have used line §3 to press that point and
instead conceded the home turf. *Verdict on F2:* mild over-concession in framing, not
in logic; it is **not** fully warranted given §3, and the paper should deploy
"belief in I-language does not entail belief in UG-object" rather than walk past it.

### F3. Is the central target the chapter's WRITTEN claim, or Reiss's intentions/psychology?

**The central target is written; the periphery psychologizes, and Section 8 attacks a
disownable position.**

On the core: the "monopoly claim" is not the author's invention. I went looking for a
fabricated paraphrase and found an accurate one. Reiss writes "no grounds" (§6), "only
the belief in Universal Grammar" (§5.3.3), and "no reason to think that data from
Japanese should be any more relevant ... than data concerning bird migration or soap
bubbles" (§5.3.1). The paper's paraphrase (p. 37) is faithful, even understated. So the
sharpest hostile line I was handed — "the monopoly claim is your paraphrase, not his
text" — collapses. Credit to the paper.

On the periphery, however, several framings attack what Reiss can disown:

- "turns colleagues into claimants and disagreement into a question of standing"
  (p. 107) reads intent into "self-identify as linguists" (§2). The paper concedes
  "That phrasing isn't the argument," which is an admission that a whole section
  (Sec. 5, Boundary Stipulation) is built on something Reiss can drop with no cost.
- "Reiss tends to run together" (abstract) and "Reiss's chapter often lets the first
  do the work of all three" (p. 63) attribute a *conflation*. Reiss would deny the
  conflation: for him the postulate that makes apples-and-planets one domain is the
  *same* postulate that licenses apple-data-bearing-on-planets — domain and transfer
  are one role, by design (the Newton analogy). The paper needs to argue that they
  *should* be separated, not assert that Reiss sloppily merged them.
- Section 8 is the clearest case. Reiss brackets public languages as "a different kind
  of thing" he is not studying (§2); the paper treats this as an "ontological
  restriction" it then refutes. That is attacking a position Reiss explicitly
  declines to hold.

*Net:* the load-bearing target is the written exclusivity claim, which is fair game and
fairly represented. But roughly a third of the paper (Sec. 5 boundary rhetoric, Sec. 8
public objects) engages framing and bracketed positions Reiss can disown, and that is
where a hostile reviewer concentrates fire.

---

## Bottom line for the author

You correctly identified a genuine over-claim in Reiss's *written* conclusion, and the
thin/thick dilemma plus the comparative-method counterexample refute its literal
universal form. But Reiss does not need the literal universal form. Quote his three
exclusivity statements, accept that your established result is the *negative* one
(UG is not the sole licence), and either supply a worked *horizontal* non-UG warrant or
stop implying you have one. Re-cast Section 8 around "bracketing is not refuting,"
quoting §2. And use Reiss's own UG-object ≠ I-language distinction (§3) instead of
conceding the I-language domain to UG. Do that and the paper survives the Reiss-defender
it is sure to draw. As written, it wins the argument it picked and loses the one Reiss
would actually make.
