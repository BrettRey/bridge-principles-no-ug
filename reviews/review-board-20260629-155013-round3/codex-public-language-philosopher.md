**1. Summary**

The paper argues that Reiss is right that crosslinguistic evidence needs a warrant, but wrong that UG is the only possible warrant: evidential travel should instead be audited by explicit projectibility profiles with targets, diagnostics, scope, and defeaters.

**2. Strengths**

- The paper reconstructs Reiss charitably before attacking him. It grants the central methodological point: categories like NPI, wh-word, segment, and sentence are theory-mediated, and crosslinguistic comparison is not licensed by the bare word “language.”
- The thin/thick UG dilemma in Sections 3 and 6 is the strongest contribution. If UG is thin, it gives only potential relevance; if thick, it becomes one auditable first-order warrant among others. This directly pressures Reiss’s claims in Section 5.3.3 and the conclusion, especially “Only the belief in Universal Grammar” and “Anyone who denies UG has no grounds.”
- The French-English *any* audit is effective. Treating French as a cue rather than a license captures what is genuinely useful in Reiss’s example while avoiding category importation. The Turkish evidentiality comparison is especially clarifying.

**3. Weaknesses**

- The paper needs to distinguish more sharply between two targets: Reiss’s local I-language claim and his broader written exclusivity claim. Reiss explicitly stipulates in Section 2 that his “linguists” are Chomskyans, but he also makes broader-sounding claims in Sections 3, 5.3.3, 5.3.8, and 6. The paper should say: if the target is the broad written monopoly claim, historical comparison and public-language ontology matter; if the target is the local I-language claim, those examples do less work.
- Section 8 only partially answers the public-language individuation worry. It usefully shifts from compact location to distributed maintenance, but it risks relabeling a self-maintaining norm in projectibility terms unless it gives identity conditions: what makes this the same public object across speakers, institutions, genres, and time? The moon rebuttal is sound against a crude transport test, but not yet a full answer to Reiss’s location/individuation challenge.
- The public-language material is connected to the paper only as an ontological flank, not as part of the crosslinguistic-transfer argument. The paper admits this in Section 8. That honesty helps, but the section still feels like a second paper unless the author either shortens it or explicitly frames it as answering Reiss’s restriction of legitimate linguistic targets to I-languages. Claims about Stainton, Santana, Pullum/Scholz, Milroy, and Agha should be verified independently; I have not checked those sources here.

**4. Key Q&A Question**

If Reiss says, “My claim was only that crosslinguistic evidence bears on *I-language* inquiry through UG, not that historical linguists, sociolinguists, or public-language theorists cannot study their own objects,” which part of your argument still refutes him rather than changing the subject?

**5. Verdict**

Revise & Resubmit: the core projectibility argument is promising and philosophically publishable, but the target, the I-language concession, and the public-language section need tightening before the paper can withstand a Reiss-style reply.

**F1. Reiss’s Likely Retreat**

The paper is only partly robust to the retreat “I meant only I-language inquiry.” If Reiss retreats to that local claim, Sections 7 and 8 lose much of their force because historical reconstruction and public standards are not primarily evidence-transfer arguments about an individual’s I-language. They defeat the broad monopoly claim, not the local Chomskyan claim.

The objection does not dissolve entirely. Sections 3 and 6 still press a serious point: even within I-language inquiry, bare UG does not by itself license a particular transfer from French to English. The warrant must specify the projected target, diagnostics, scope, and defeaters. But the paper should make this division explicit: the local reply is the thin/thick dilemma plus the *any* audit; the global reply is historical comparison plus public/distributed objects.

**F2. Over-Concession**

The sentence in Section 2 is potentially ambiguous but not fatal: “successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry” does not strictly concede that I-language inquiry as such requires UG. Still, Reiss gives the author a useful distinction that the paper should exploit. In Section 3, Reiss says “belief in I-language does not entail belief in UG-object.” That means the paper should not let readers infer that mental-grammar inquiry itself needs UG.

A cleaner formulation would be: even if some Chomskyan I-language warrants succeed, I-language inquiry as mental-grammar inquiry need not presuppose UG-object, and UG does not thereby become the sole license for evidential travel. The current sentence is defensible, but it should be revised to block the over-concession reading.

**F3. Written Claim or Psychology?**

The paper’s strongest target is Reiss’s written exclusivity claim, not his intentions or psychology. “Monopoly claim” is justified because the chapter repeatedly says UG uniquely licenses crosslinguistic evidential travel. Reiss can disown some global implications, but he cannot easily disown the textual pattern.

That said, phrases like “turns colleagues into claimants” are rhetorically risky. Reiss can answer that he was merely stipulating a technical use of “linguists,” not denying anyone’s professional standing. The paper should keep the boundary-policing point, but make it secondary: the problem is not Reiss’s tone or psychology; it is that a local stipulation plus an I-language ontology is made to support an exclusivity claim wider than the argument earns.
