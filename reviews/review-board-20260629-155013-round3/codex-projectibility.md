**1. Summary**  
The paper argues that Reiss is right that crosslinguistic evidence needs a warrant, but wrong that UG has a monopoly on such warrants: the relevant standard is whether a bridge principle makes a target projectible for a declared purpose.

**Projectibility Audit**

| Check | Status | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Framing | GREEN | The abstract and Introduction frame the contribution as a “projectibility test”: target, profile, diagnostics, defeaters, and scope. |
| Profile Definition | GREEN | Section 4 defines projection as inference from observed to expected features, not merely clustering. |
| Mechanisms | GREEN | Mechanisms are mostly treated as stabilizers of projection, especially in Sections 6-8. |
| Worked Examples | GREEN/YELLOW | French *any* and historical reconstruction are strong; typological comparanda are mostly deferred. |
| Conclusion | GREEN/YELLOW | It cashes out historical and public-standard projections, but should more explicitly enumerate what each bridge principle predicts or fails to predict. |

Overall: projectibility is doing structural work, not decorative work. This is not a mechanisms-first paper.

**2. Strengths**

1. The paper is charitable before it is critical. It grants Reiss’s strongest point: categories are theory-mediated, and crosslinguistic evidence does not travel for free. That tracks Reiss’s own claim that UG “allows data from each language to bear on the analysis of all languages” and his Newton analogy.

2. The central projectibility framework is genuinely epistemic. Section 4 keeps asking: what is the target, what is the profile, what licenses the projection, and what would defeat it? That is the right Boyd-style payoff: “a profile, stabilized by mechanisms, projectible for a purpose.”

3. The French *any* audit is the strongest part. It does not merely say “French and English differ”; it reconstructs the inference and demotes French evidence to cue status unless English-internal diagnostics and a support profile are supplied. This directly targets Reiss’s claim that “Only the belief in Universal Grammar” licenses the French-English move.

**3. Weaknesses**

1. The paper is only partly robust against Reiss’s likely retreat: “I meant I-language inquiry, not all of linguistics.” The written chapter gives the paper a real target, especially Reiss’s conclusion that “Anyone who denies UG has no grounds” for importing categories. But if Reiss retreats to a local claim about Chomskyan I-language inquiry, Sections 7-8 become less decisive because historical reconstruction and public standards are not obviously I-language warrants. Action: add an explicit fallback argument: even restricted to mental-grammar inquiry, UG-object is not logically required for all legitimate I-language warrants.

2. The sentence near the end of Section 2 risks over-concession: “successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry…” It does not strictly concede that I-language inquiry requires UG, but it can be read that way. That would be too generous, because Reiss himself says “belief in I-language does not entail belief in UG-object,” and explicitly allows someone to believe in mental grammars without UG-object. Action: revise to “successful UG-shaped warrants for some Chomskyan I-language inferences…” and explicitly separate mental-grammar realism from UG nativism.

3. The conclusion should make the projection payoffs more explicit. It currently does this well for historical reconstruction and public standards, but less so for typological comparanda and functional recurrence. Action: close with a compact mapping: UG-shaped I-language warrant predicts X; historical descent predicts Y; public standard predicts Z; French *any* currently predicts nothing beyond cue-level without further support.

**4. Key Q&A Question**

If Reiss says, “I only meant that crosslinguistic evidence for I-language theories requires UG, not that all linguistic inquiry requires UG,” what is your non-UG bridge principle for I-language inquiry itself?

**5. Verdict**

Revise & Resubmit: the projectibility framework is strong and central, but the paper needs to tighten its fallback against the local I-language retreat and avoid conceding that I-language inquiry itself requires UG.

**F1. Robustness to Reiss’s Local Retreat**

Partly robust, but not fully. The paper has a target because Reiss’s written claims are broader than a modest local claim: he says the UG postulate licenses treating each language’s data as relevant to all others, and that those who deny UG lack grounds for importing analytic categories. So the “monopoly claim” is not invented.

However, if Reiss retreats to “only I-language inquiry,” the public-language material no longer directly refutes him; it shows pluralism about linguistic objects, not a non-UG warrant for I-language transfer. Historical comparison also risks being dismissed as about diachronic public/external forms rather than I-language. The paper should therefore add a local argument: Reiss’s own UG-object/I-language distinction shows that mental grammar inquiry need not entail UG-object.

**F2. Does the Paper Over-Concede?**

Yes, slightly. The Section 2 sentence is dangerous. It can sound as if the paper grants that I-language inquiry, as such, needs UG-shaped warrants. That concession is not warranted by Reiss’s own chapter.

Reiss explicitly distinguishes I-language from UG-object: “belief in I-language does not entail belief in UG-object.” He even says one could believe mental grammars arise through “general learning mechanisms” without language-specific categories. The paper should exploit this. It should concede only that UG may provide warrants for some Chomskyan I-language projects, not that mental-grammar inquiry requires UG.

**F3. Written Target or Reiss’s Psychology?**

The central target is the chapter’s written exclusivity claim, and that target is legitimate. Reiss writes that only UG licenses the French-English argument and concludes that denying UG leaves one without grounds for crosslinguistic category import. “Monopoly claim” is therefore fair.

The risky material is rhetorical, especially “turns colleagues into claimants.” Reiss can disown that as a complaint about tone or psychology rather than argument. The paper should keep the pressure on the written exclusivity language: “Only the belief in Universal Grammar…,” “licenses us…,” and “Anyone who denies UG…”. Preserve “monopoly claim” if desired, but anchor it each time in those textual claims rather than in Reiss’s intentions.
