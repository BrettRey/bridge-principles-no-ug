**1. One-Sentence Summary**

The paper argues that Reiss is right that crosslinguistic evidence needs a warrant, but wrong that only Chomskyan UG can provide one: comparison requires explicit bridge principles with targets, support profiles, scope, and defeaters.

**2. Strengths**

1. The paper charitably identifies Reiss’s strongest point. It does not caricature armchair linguistics as non-empirical; it grants that linguistic categories are theory-mediated and that evidence does not travel merely because two things are called “languages.” This matches Reiss’s own claim that UG lets “data from each language... bear on the analysis of all languages.”

2. Section 4 usefully separates comparison, classification, and evidential transfer. This is exactly the right Haspelmath/Croft-style discipline: comparative concepts can organize comparison without becoming language-internal descriptive categories, and classification of English `any` as one or two items cannot be read off the French contrast.

3. The French `any` audit is the paper’s strongest applied section. It correctly treats French translation evidence as a cue, not a licence. The real burden falls on English-internal diagnostics plus a support profile. That is methodologically sound and avoids turning one-off translation contrasts into typological projection.

**3. Weaknesses**

1. The paper should sharpen its target against Reiss’s likely retreat. Reiss can plausibly say he stipulated `linguists` to mean Chomskyans and that he is defending I-language inquiry. The paper still has a target because Reiss also writes: “Anyone who denies UG has no grounds for importing analytic categories from one language to the study of another.” But the paper should make clear that its objection is to this written exclusivity claim, not to the modest claim that UG can guide Chomskyan I-language inquiry.

2. The Section 2 concession risks over-granting. The sentence “successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry...” could be read as conceding that I-language inquiry as such needs UG. That concession is not warranted by Reiss’s own chapter, since he explicitly says: “One could, in principle, be a psychologist who believes that humans have mental grammars, without believing in UG-object” and “belief in I-language does not entail belief in UG-object.” Revise to say that some UG-shaped warrants may succeed for some I-language analyses, but mental-grammar inquiry is not identical to UG-object inquiry.

3. The Haspelmath/Croft move is correct but underdeveloped. The paper says comparative concepts and Radical Construction Grammar provide non-UG discipline, but its only fully successful non-UG transfer case is historical-comparative reconstruction. That defeats UG monopoly, but it does not yet give a robust case of horizontal typological projection across unrelated languages. Add explicit criteria for genuine typological projection: independent sampling, coding rules, genealogical/areal controls, contact and borrowing checks, translation-artifact controls, and held-out predictions. Haspelmath/Croft sources themselves would need verification if developed further.

**4. Key Q&A Question**

If French `any` evidence is only a cue until English-internal diagnostics do the licensing, what would count as a successful non-UG typological bridge across unrelated languages, rather than merely disciplined comparison plus language-internal confirmation?

**5. Verdict**

Revise & Resubmit: the core argument is strong, but the paper needs to narrow the target, avoid over-conceding I-language-to-UG dependence, and clarify the difference between historical transfer and genuine typological projection.

**F1. Reiss’s Likely Retreat To I-Language Inquiry**

The paper is partly robust, but should prepare the fallback more explicitly. If Reiss retreats to “I meant only I-language inquiry,” then the public-language and broad-field parts lose some force against him locally. But the objection does not dissolve, because Reiss’s written claim is broader than “UG is useful inside my framework.” He says that “only the belief in Universal Grammar” licenses the French-English move, and concludes that anyone denying UG lacks grounds for importing categories across languages.

So the paper still has a target if it says: even for I-language inquiry, Reiss has not shown that UG is the only possible warrant. If he retreats further to “within my Chomskyan program, UG is my adopted postulate,” then the monopoly objection dissolves, but so does Reiss’s exclusivity claim. The paper should force that choice.

**F2. Does The Paper Over-Concede?**

Yes, slightly. The sentence near the end of Section 2 does not logically concede that I-language inquiry requires UG, but it can pragmatically sound that way. This matters because Reiss himself distinguishes mental grammar from UG-object. His chapter explicitly says one could believe in mental grammars without believing in UG-object.

So the concession should be narrowed. It is warranted to concede that Reiss-style Chomskyan I-language inquiry uses UG as a postulate and can generate empirical warrants. It is not warranted to concede that I-language inquiry itself requires UG. A better formulation would be: “Even where UG-shaped warrants succeed for some I-language analyses, that does not show that every I-language warrant must be UG-shaped, still less that every legitimate linguistic target must be an I-language target.”

**F3. Written Monopoly Claim Or Reiss’s Psychology?**

The paper’s strongest target is the chapter’s written exclusivity claim, not Reiss’s intentions. “Monopoly claim” is fair if anchored to Reiss’s own wording: “Only the belief in Universal Grammar...” and “Anyone who denies UG has no grounds...” That is enough.

The risk is in formulations like “turns colleagues into claimants.” Reiss can disown that as a claim about his psychology or intentions, saying he merely stipulated terminology. The paper should keep the point textual: the phrase “self-identify as linguists,” combined with the later exclusivity claim, has a boundary-policing effect. The argument does not need to infer motive.
