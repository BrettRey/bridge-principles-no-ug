# Projectibility Reviewer — Round 3

**Paper:** *Bridge Principles without UG: Projectibility, Crosslinguistic Evidence, and Linguistic Ontology* (Reynolds)
**Reply target:** Reiss, *Research Methods in Armchair Linguistics* (Aug 2025 draft, Oxford Handbook of Philosophy of Linguistics)
**Reviewer mandate:** Boyd's slogan — "a profile, stabilised by mechanisms, projectible for a purpose." My sole concern is whether projectibility is the load-bearing epistemic payoff or a decorative overlay on a mechanism/clustering argument.

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

Crosslinguistic evidence needs an auditable warrant ("bridge principle"), but Universal Grammar holds no monopoly on supplying one: a projectibility audit (target / profile / diagnostics / defeaters) shows that historical descent, social-practical maintenance, and typological function can license evidential travel that UG does not own.

## 2. Strengths (specific)

1. **Projectibility is genuinely load-bearing, not ornamental.** The framework does argumentative work the prose could not do without it. The thin/thick dilemma (Sec. 3, "A thin UG postulate can't license the monopoly; a thick UG theory is one auditable warrant among others") only lands because projectibility has already separated a *domain-defining postulate* from a *first-order transfer warrant with target, support, scope, and failure conditions*. The schema in Sec. 4 ("if source features S identify profile P under conditions C, then target expectation Q is licensed until defeaters D appear") is a projection rule, and the cases instantiate it. This is the right structure, and it is rare in an LLM-influenced draft to see the mechanism subordinated to the projection rather than the reverse.

2. **The cases cash out in concrete projections, not just classifications.** The historical section delivers a real induction: a correspondence set (Latin *pater*, Greek *patēr*, Sanskrit *pitar-*, Gothic *fadar*) supports a reconstruction, and "Evidence from Gothic can bear on the reconstruction, and the reconstruction can bear on the analysis of the Latin, Greek, and Sanskrit forms, because transfer is mediated by a causal-historical profile" (Sec. 7). The varieties case is explicitly bidirectional and predictive ("Knowing enough about the conditioning state should help predict likely forms, and hearing the forms should help infer the conditioning state," Sec. 8). The standard-English case generates *differential* predictions (style-guide evidence bears on edited prose but "doesn't support the same projection about spontaneous speech"). These are projections with declared scope and defeaters, which is exactly the payoff Boyd's third clause demands.

3. **Source-grounded engagement with Reiss, in the spirit of Rapoport's Rules.** Sec. 2 concedes Reiss's strongest point fairly ("Good armchair work isn't mere introspection... It's a form of theory-mediated empirical reasoning"), and the French-*any* reconstruction (Sec. 6) keeps Reiss's useful inference while demoting only its licence. The attribution of the monopoly is anchored in Reiss's *written* conclusion rather than a strawman (see F3). The complications the paper credits to Reiss are actually in his footnote 15 ("negative concord and negative quantifiers, dialect variation in French including a mismatch between standard written French and spoken varieties") — accurately reported.

## 3. Weaknesses (specific and actionable)

1. **The paper's *self-description* of projectibility tilts custodial (veto) when its *cases* are productive (prediction).** Sec. 4 calls projectibility "a second-order discipline for evaluating such warrants, not a rival language faculty." That framing makes projectibility a gatekeeper that polices transfers, whereas Goodman's projectibility is a *first-order inductive licence* — what lets you predict unobserved features from observed ones. The first half of the paper (Secs. 3, 5, 6) is dominated by demotion ("demote French to a cue," "the inference has been built by assuming the destination classification"), so a reader can finish Sec. 6 thinking projectibility is mainly a debunking tool. The productive use only arrives in Secs. 7–8. **Fix:** front-load one *positive* projection in Sec. 4 (the corpus/register micro-example at the end of Sec. 4 already does this — promote it), so the predictive yield leads and the veto follows. State once, early, that the payoff of declaring a bridge is that it *generates and bounds inductions*, and the demotion of UG's monopoly is a corollary, not the headline.

2. **The conclusion under-delivers the public-standard projection it earned in Sec. 8.** Sec. 9 gives the historical case a concrete projection ("inherited correspondences are expected under causal-genealogical scope") and the French case a clean demotion, but the public-standard and typological targets are left at the meta-level ("declare the projections they support and the failures they accept"). The paper already has a crisp concrete projection for the standard (style-guide evidence predicts the treatment of *who*/*whom*, *between you and I*, *its*/*it's* in edited academic prose but not in child speech). Pull it into the conclusion. A projectibility venue will read a meta-level conclusion ("declare your projections") as the audit checklist reasserting itself in place of an actual induction.

3. **The Sec. 2 concession is worded more generously than the rest of the paper can afford (see F2).** "Successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry" passes without flagging that, by the paper's own thin/thick dilemma, "successful" must mean a *thick, audited* UG theory functioning as one first-order warrant — not the bare postulate. As written, a reader can take it to grant that the UG postulate licenses transfer within I-language work, which is the very monopoly Secs. 3 and 6 deny. This is a wording risk, not a substantive contradiction, but at a philosophy-of-linguistics venue it is the sentence a hostile reader will quote back.

## 4. Key question for Q&A

You frame projectibility as "a second-order discipline for evaluating warrants." Name one concrete, *novel* projection that a non-UG bridge principle licenses and that a UG-monopoly view would get wrong — not a demotion of Reiss's example, but a positive prediction your framework generates and UG-based linguistics would miss. If you can, projectibility is the payoff; if all you can produce is demotions, it is a veto dressed as a faculty.

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit (minor-to-moderate).** The argument is sound and projectibility is load-bearing; the revisions are targeted — rebalance the custodial framing toward the predictive payoff, tighten the Sec. 2 concession, and carry the public-standard projection into the conclusion.

## FOCUS QUESTIONS

### F1. Robustness to Reiss's retreat ("I meant only I-language inquiry, not all of linguistics")

**Yes, the paper survives the retreat, and projectibility is what supplies the surviving target — but the anti-retreat move should be stated outright rather than left implicit.**

Two things matter. First, the retreat is not available on the *text*. Reiss's written conclusion is unrestricted: "Anyone who denies UG has no grounds for importing analytic categories from one language to the study of another" (Conclusion), and the body generalises the same way — "the postulate of UG expands the empirical base for an analysis of English to include data from all human languages... without UG, each data point is actually only relevant to a single language" (EAD 1), and on the French case, "What licenses this reasoning? Only the belief in Universal Grammar allows us to construct this kind of argument" (EAD 3). None of these is hedged to "within I-language inquiry"; they are claims about evidential travel *as such*. So the retreat is a different paper from the one written (this is F3's point too).

Second, even if Reiss retreats, projectibility hands the paper a target that does not depend on him staying put: the historical-comparative case is crosslinguistic *importing* (Gothic evidence bears on a reconstruction that bears on Latin/Greek/Sanskrit) that is *not* I-language inquiry yet is uncontroversially linguistics, and the licence is "causal-historical rather than UG-theoretic" (Sec. 7). The retreat would force Reiss either to deny that historical reconstruction imports analytic categories across languages, or to concede it has its own non-UG warrant — which is the paper's thesis. Projectibility is doing the work here because it reframes the dispute as "which target, licensed by which profile," so I-language inquiry becomes *one* target-plus-bridge among several rather than the precondition of comparison. The retreat shrinks Reiss's claim to a domain where it is true and uninteresting, and the paper's positive cases live outside that domain.

**Actionable:** add one sentence near the end of Sec. 7 or in the conclusion making the anti-retreat explicit: "Even restricted to I-language inquiry, the monopoly claim still fails, because historical reconstruction is crosslinguistic importing that is not I-language inquiry and that Reiss's written conclusion covers." Right now this is derivable from the text but never named, and naming it closes the most predictable line of defence.

### F2. Over-concession at "successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry"? Is I-language a target or a bridge, and does the paper keep that straight?

**The substance is fine; the wording over-concedes, and the projectibility distinction the paper needs is *target vs. bridge*, which it keeps straight in the framework but blurs in this one sentence.**

In the paper's own ontology (Sec. 4): a **target** is what the inference is *about* — and "an individual mental grammar" is listed explicitly as a target. A **bridge** is the scoped licence from diagnostics to target through a profile; UG is "one possible first-order filling of the schema." So I-language = target; UG = bridge. The Sec. 2 sentence parses correctly on those terms: *UG-shaped warrants* (bridge) *for I-language inquiry* (target). The paper does keep the categories straight at the framework level.

The over-concession is that "successful UG-shaped warrants for I-language inquiry" grants success without the qualifier the paper's own thin/thick dilemma requires. By Sec. 3, a *thin* UG postulate licenses no transfer at all, and a *thick* UG theory is "a first-order warrant with its own target, support, scope, and failure conditions... one auditable warrant among others." So the only sense in which UG-shaped warrants are "successful" is the thick, audited sense — in which case they are not *UG's* success but the success of a specified theory under the same projectibility discipline applied to everyone else. The Sec. 2 sentence omits that qualifier and so reads as conceding that the UG postulate, qua postulate, warrants I-language transfer. That is exactly the monopoly the paper exists to deny.

There is also a deeper trap the paper should name. For Reiss, "I-language built from shared UG primitives" is *simultaneously* the ontology of the target and the bridge — the shared-primitives assumption is both what the objects are and what licenses transfer between them. The cleanest projectibility move is to refuse that fusion: insist that I-language is *only* a target (an individual mental grammar), and that whatever does transfer work is a separable bridge (a thick, defeasible UG theory) answerable to the same audit as historical descent or social maintenance. The paper does this everywhere except the Sec. 2 sentence, where "I-language inquiry" quietly carries the bridge along with the target.

**Actionable fix:** revise to, e.g., "successful *thick* UG warrants — substantive theories with stated diagnostics and defeaters — meet the audit for I-language targets; that success is one warrant under the same discipline, not the bare postulate licensing transfer, and not a reason every legitimate target must be an I-language target." This keeps target (I-language) and bridge (thick UG theory) apart and removes the sentence a hostile reader would otherwise quote.

### F3. Is the central target the chapter's *written* exclusivity claim or Reiss's intentions?

**The written claim, and it is correctly quoted — with a few intentional-sounding locutions that should be converted to textual ones.**

The paper anchors the monopoly in Reiss's text: the conclusion's "Anyone who denies UG has no grounds for importing analytic categories from one language to the study of another" is the load-bearing quotation, and Sec. 1 paraphrases it accurately ("denying UG leaves one without grounds for importing analytic categories from one language into another. That's the monopoly claim at issue"). The paper is methodologically protected because the thin/thick dilemma covers both readings of Reiss's *intent*: whether he meant a thin postulate or a thick theory, neither delivers the monopoly. So the argument does not depend on divining intentions — a real strength.

The lapses are stylistic, not structural. The abstract says the test "separates three questions Reiss tends to run together," and Sec. 3 says "Reiss's chapter often lets the first do the work of all three." "Tends to" and "often lets" are dispositional/intentional locutions; they attribute a habit of reasoning rather than citing the text. Each is backstopped by quotation elsewhere, so the target stays written, but the phrasing invites the "you're psychoanalysing me" rejoinder.

**Actionable:** convert these to textual claims — "the chapter runs these three together at secs. 1, 5–6" rather than "Reiss tends to run together." Keep the dilemma framing prominent precisely because it makes the dispute intention-proof: whatever Reiss meant, the written claim over-reaches.
