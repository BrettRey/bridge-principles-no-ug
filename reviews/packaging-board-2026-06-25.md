# Packaging Review Board: Bridge Principles without UG

Date: 2026-06-25  
Target: `main.tex`  
Board source: HPC book packaging board (`words-that-wont-hold-still/notes/advisory-board.md`)  
Reviewers: Deirdre McCloskey, Wendy Lesser, Carl Zimmer, Edward Tufte, Howard Becker, Errol Morris  
Additional audit: Projectibility Reviewer  
Follow-up inputs: Claude rhetorical pass and GPT Pro rhetorical pass supplied by Brett

## Executive Verdict

The board sees a strong argument with a packaging problem. The core claim is stable: Reiss is right that crosslinguistic evidence needs a bridge principle, but wrong that Chomskyan UG is the only possible bridge. The strongest argumentative hinge is the thin/thick UG dilemma: a thin UG postulate cannot license particular transfers; a thick UG theory becomes one auditable bridge among others.

The main weakness is staging. The paper still asks readers to absorb too much projectibility taxonomy before they have felt the live evidential problem. The French `any` case and the historical-comparative case make the argument concrete, but they arrive after the reader has already crossed a glossary of targets, profiles, diagnostics, ordering relations, stabilizers, scope conditions, defeaters, cues, scoped bridges, and robust bridges.

The follow-up reviews sharpen the diagnosis: the paper makes the reader work harder than the argument requires. Three things drag: the apparatus arrives before the gap has been felt, the disciplinary-boundary material is set up twice, and the seven-part audit formula is repeated until it risks going numb.

Verdict: minor-to-major packaging revision, not major argumentative repair.

## Consensus Strengths

1. **The concessionary opening is doing real work.** All reviewers praised `What Reiss Gets Right`. It makes the paper sound like an insider critique: theory-mediated evidence, armchair method, realism, and crosslinguistic warrant are accepted before the monopoly claim is attacked.

2. **The thin/thick UG dilemma is the cleanest argumentative move.** Multiple reviewers singled out the sentence: “A thin UG postulate can’t license the monopoly; a thick UG theory is one auditable bridge among others.” Protect this.

3. **The French `any` audit is the best scene.** Reviewers repeatedly described it as the point where the machinery becomes visible. “Translation into French is a diagnostic cue ... not because it transfers a French category into English” was identified as one of the paper’s most portable formulations.

4. **Historical comparison is the right positive counterexample.** The Latin/Greek/Sanskrit/Gothic miniature gives the reader a tactile handle on non-UG evidential travel. It shows a bridge with diagnostics, scope, and defeaters rather than merely asserting pluralism.

5. **Projectibility is genuinely doing epistemic work.** The dedicated projectibility audit returned Yellow-Green: the framework is not decorative, especially in the French audit and conclusion. The remaining issue is uneven explicitness across cases.

## Consensus Weaknesses

1. **The apparatus arrives before the reader feels why it is needed.** The introduction gives the target, the stipulation, the monopoly claim, the two faces of the argument, and projectibility before the reader sees a transfer attempt in motion. Section 3 then adds the full apparatus before Section 4 diagnoses Reiss's thin/thick shift. The strongest version of this criticism recommends moving the monopoly diagnosis before the projectibility framework. The lighter version keeps the order but adds a roadmap, cuts Section 3 by roughly a third, and defers the comparanda/realization material to the `any` audit.

2. **The projectibility section is over-labeled.** The new section is needed, but its current form risks becoming a glossary wall. Tufte’s concrete fix is a compact displayed schema with rows for cue, scoped bridge, and robust bridge, and columns for target, diagnostic, ordering/stabilizer, scope, and defeater. A plain-language sentence should precede the terminology: a bridge principle says why these data matter for that claim, under these conditions, until these defeaters appear.

3. **The boundary-stipulation material is built twice.** Section 1 introduces the typography and definitions; Section 5 reintroduces them and restates the same contrast. Section 5's genuinely new material is the `self-identify as linguists` presentation-order point and the reflexive bridge demand: why should conclusions about Reiss's `linguists' project onto \term{linguists} as such? Fold the reflexive demand into the monopoly diagnosis, keep the `self-identify` beat, and avoid making Section 5 carry "Disciplinary Ontology" when the ontology payoff arrives in Section 8.

4. **The seven-part audit formula is repeated too often.** The list target/profile/diagnostics/ordering relation/stabilizer/scope/defeaters appears in several sections and again in the conclusion. Define it once, use it fully where needed, and then refer to "the audit," "the profile," or "support and failure conditions." Repetition currently reads like anxiety that the reader has forgotten the framework.

5. **Some sharp lines sound prosecutorial before the evidence has earned them.** Reviewers independently flagged “password,” “kills the monopoly claim,” the forced dilemma about historical reconstruction, and especially “almost literally lunatic.” GPT Pro would keep “password” because it targets the Newton analogy; Claude and the packaging board are more cautious. All agree the lunar pun should go or be softened.

6. **The public-language section may feel like a second paper.** Reviewers agree it answers a real ontological half of Reiss’s restriction, but it needs a tighter transition and fewer same-grain examples. The Apollo/NASA reductio is vivid; the `its`/`it’s` and `who`/`whom` cases are concrete; together they may be more than a short response can digest unless one is clearly made the controlling example.

7. **Non-specialist footholds are still missing in a few places.** The `any` section should give one plain example of polarity-dependent `any` and free-choice `any`, for example `I didn't eat any cookies` versus `choose any card`. `Downward-entailing` needs a gloss or delayed introduction. Section 7 should name Grimm's Law. Section 8 should gloss `enregisterment`. The first use of `I-language` should briefly say individual internal grammar.

8. **Horizontal transfer remains the predictable Q&A vulnerability.** Historical comparison kills the monopoly claim by giving a vertical non-UG bridge. It does not supply a robust horizontal bridge for unrelated languages. The paper already admits this, but reviewers want the limitation framed as principled modesty rather than a retreat.

## Projectibility-Specific Findings

Projectibility is not merely ornamental, but the cases are uneven.

- French `any`: strong. It declares target, diagnostics, support grade, and defeaters well.
- Historical comparison: strong but should explicitly state support grade and projected payoff.
- Public standards: persuasive but should push prediction forward earlier, not just object-realism.
- Human-language domain contrast: currently closer to a cluster-and-stabilizer list than a projectibility claim. It should say more directly what the human-language profile predicts that chess, Python, and tango do not.

Recommended projectibility revisions:

1. Add a compact profile-specification sentence for each major case.
2. Revise the domain/chess/Python/tango passage to state concrete projections and defeaters.
3. Add one concluding sentence naming the strongest successful projection: historical-comparative bridges predict inherited correspondences under causal-genealogical scope, while French `any` remains cue-level without independent English-internal support.

## Contradictions

1. **Keep or move the projectibility framework?** The board agrees the framework is necessary, but differs on structure. Claude's strongest recommendation is to move the monopoly diagnosis before the framework, because the thin/thick dilemma can be stated in plain English and creates demand for the apparatus. GPT Pro would keep the current order because projectibility needs to be introduced before the boundary and audit sections depend on it. Lesser and Zimmer want the live case before the taxonomy. Tufte would keep the framework but compress it into a displayed schema. Best synthesis: either move Section 4 before Section 3, or keep the order only if Section 3 is cut and made case-driven.

2. **Sharpness versus trust.** The board accepts the desire to skewer Reiss’s argumentative moves, but sees a trust cost in a few flourishes. The strongest path is selective sharpness: keep the thin/thick dilemma and the cue/passport distinction; soften or better earn the moon pun and courtroom-style forced dilemmas.

3. **Public ontology: necessary or diluting?** Some reviewers see the public-language section as essential because Reiss’s restriction is ontological as well as evidential. Others see it as a second paper. The compromise is to keep it but make the first paragraph explicitly say why ontology belongs in this response, then reduce example proliferation.

4. **How hard to cut Section 8?** Claude recommends cutting the second public-standard example cluster because it restates the `its`/`it's` logic. GPT Pro would keep at least one beyond-orthography cluster because it blocks the objection that public standards are merely spelling conventions. Best synthesis: keep one short beyond-orthography sentence, not a second developed cluster.

## Prioritized Revision List

1. **Decide the Section 3/Section 4 order.** Highest-value change: move the monopoly diagnosis before the projectibility framework. If that feels too disruptive, keep the current order but add a roadmap at the end of the introduction, cut Section 3 by roughly a third, move comparanda/realization and the three operations into Section 6, and put a plain-language bridge sentence before the terminology.

2. **Rebuild the projectibility section around a cue/scoped/robust schema.** Use one compact grid or prose equivalent. Let one example carry the reader through the taxonomy. Move the bridge-principle formula earlier because it clarifies the whole framework.

3. **Collapse the boundary-stipulation redundancy.** Let the introduction announce the typography convention in two sentences. Move the reflexive projection demand into the monopoly diagnosis. Keep the `self-identify as linguists` observation as a short beat, not a second full setup.

4. **Stop re-enumerating the full audit list.** After the first full statement, use compressed references such as "the audit," "support profile," "scope," and "failure conditions." In the conclusion, replace the full list with something like "declare the target, support profile, and failure conditions."

5. **Add a roadmap and non-specialist footholds.** The roadmap should tell readers why the paper moves from Reiss to projectibility to `any` to historical comparison to public standards. Add: an `any` example pair, a gloss for `downward-entailing`, first-use gloss for `I-language`, Grimm's Law by name, and a gloss for `enregisterment`.

6. **Cool or earn the highest-risk skewers.** Reconsider “kills the monopoly claim,” the historical-comparative forced dilemma, and especially “almost literally lunatic.” `Password` can stay if the Newton setup earns it. Keep the sharp lines that do analytical work: the thin/thick dilemma, the local-stipulation/global-work line, and the cue/passport contrast.

7. **Make the horizontal-transfer limitation explicit and controlled.** State that the paper defeats the monopoly claim, not that it has supplied a robust non-UG bridge for all unrelated-language transfer.

8. **Add profile-spec sentences to historical comparison and public standards.** Mark support grade, projection, scope, and defeaters as explicitly as the French `any` section does.

9. **Tighten the public-language section.** Keep Apollo/NASA only if the Reiss moon example is staged enough to earn the reductio. Cut or soften the lunar pun. Reduce the Armstrong list to the strongest examples. Keep one short beyond-orthography sentence so the public-standard example does not look like mere spelling.

10. **Make final line-level and source checks.** Replace or gloss `n-word behaviour` to avoid a false parse; fix spacing glitches around macros in the PDF; check Oxford spelling consistency; decide whether the acknowledgement should say Claude Opus generically or Opus 4.x; verify Reiss, Hoeksema, French `rien`, Fortson, Stainton, Santana, Pullum, Milroy, and Agha before submission.

## Review-Board Self-Check

The convergence is unusually clean, so it should not be mistaken for field consensus. The packaging board roles are correlated: all six are primed to notice staging, concrete handles, tone, and reader trust. Still, the convergence is not empty smoothing. The reviewers independently named the same sections and even the same sentences: the front-loaded framework, the French `any` case, historical comparison, the moon pun, and the horizontal-transfer vulnerability. Treat this as a high-confidence packaging diagnosis, not as evidence that the philosophical or linguistic field would converge on the same verdict.
