## Review: Projectibility-Profile Lens

*Reviewer's note on grounding: I read `main.tex` in full for this review. I have **not** read `reiss2026armchair` or re-read `Goodman1955` during this review; claims that depend on either are flagged below. Characterizations of Haspelmath, Croft, Stainton, and Santana are taken from the paper's own descriptions and not independently verified.*

---

### 1. One-sentence summary

Crosslinguistic inference does require bridge principles, but Reiss is wrong that only Chomskyan UG can supply one: a projectibility account (specify the projection target, the profile that supports the inference, its diagnostics, ordering relation, stabilizers, scope, and defeaters) licenses comparison across a plural ontology of linguistic targets.

### 2. Strengths

- **The bridge schema is genuinely inferential, not decorative.** The core formula in §"Projectibility as an Alternative Licence" ("if source features S identify profile P under conditions C, then target expectation Q is licensed until defeaters D appear") frames the contribution as projection: a category earns its keep by licensing an expectation Q with stated defeat conditions. This is projectibility doing real framing work, exactly the move the paper needs, and it is stated crisply.
- **Stabilizers are correctly subordinated to projection.** The paper avoids the standard trap of presenting mechanisms as the payoff: "Acquisition pressure, processing bias, diachronic pathway, communicative recurrence, social uptake, or institutional maintenance may stabilize a profile... None of those is a magic pass. Each has to explain why observed source properties and projected target properties are co-available in that case" (§"Projectibility as an Alternative Licence"). The order/stabilization split ("order and stabilization can come apart") is sharp and right.
- **Profile/diagnostic distinction and self-demotion are the best ideas here.** "A coding rule, a corpus cue, or a judgement contrast may locate a profile without explaining why the projected property follows" (§"...Alternative Licence") states the distinction correctly: diagnostics locate, the profile explains. And the demand that "a serious bridge principle should be able to demote itself: localize to a target, lower its support grade, or reject transfer when the expected projection fails" is a distinctive and useful piece of apparatus.

### 3. Weaknesses (sharp, actionable)

1. **The schema is asserted but never run end-to-end.** Nowhere does the paper fill in S, P, C, Q, D with concrete linguistic content and walk a single prediction from licence to defeat. The varieties passage comes closest ("Knowing enough about the conditioning state should help predict likely forms, and hearing the forms should help infer the conditioning state," §"Public and Distributed Linguistic Objects"), but it stays at "should help predict." Without one worked instance, projectibility reads as a checklist you apply rather than an engine that generates a result. This is the central gap for my lens.

2. **"Support grade" is load-bearing but unspecified.** Demotion is defined partly as "lower its support grade," and the pluralism rests on some bridges being "weak, local, or task-specific." But the paper never says what the grades are, what evidence moves a bridge between them, or how to compare support across two candidate bridges for the same target. Support is precisely what separates a strong bridge from a permissive one, the very complaint lodged against UG, so leaving it as a gesture weakens the reflexive argument.

3. **The ordering relation, the Goodmanian heart, is the least developed element.** "Non-accidental co-availability" (§"...Alternative Licence") is what makes a profile projectible rather than grue-like, yet the only concrete ordering relation offered is "formal dependency," and only for the grammar-internal case. For public, typological, and usage targets the paper names stabilizers but never says what establishes the *ordering*. This is exactly where Goodman's riddle bites (why does this co-availability project?), and `Goodman1955` is name-checked with a gloss but his actual problem is never engaged. The apparatus currently rests on an unexplained primitive.

4. **Ontic/epistemic slip in the profile definition.** "A \term{profile} is the worldly pattern. Diagnostics, ordering relations, stabilizers, scope conditions, and demotion rules are parts of the profile specification" (§"...Alternative Licence") files worldly constituents (ordering relations, stabilizers) and methodological instruments (diagnostics, demotion rules, declared scope) under one heading. But your own profile/diagnostic distinction needs diagnostics to be epistemic instruments distinct from the worldly thing that explains. Keep the ontic profile (pattern + ordering + stabilizers, in the world) separate from the epistemic specification (diagnostics, support grades, demotion rules we adopt), or the distinction you rely on elsewhere blurs.

5. **The monopoly attribution to Reiss is the hinge of the paper and is asserted, not quoted.** *(Source-grounding flag: I have not read `reiss2026armchair`.)* The whole critique turns on Reiss claiming UG is the *only* licence for evidential travel (§"The Monopoly Claim": "only Universal Grammar licenses that evidential travel"). If Reiss in fact makes the weaker claim (UG is *a* bridge, or the standard one, not the necessary and exclusive one), the paper refutes a position he doesn't hold. Rapoport's Rules also bear here: reconstruct Reiss's bridge in his own strongest terms before faulting its under-specification. The strongest version of this paper quotes the exclusivity claim verbatim.

### 4. Add / cut / rewrite before this is a polished full draft

- **Add one fully worked bridge** (e.g., an NPI licensing claim, or the register-conditioning case already gestured at) with real S, P, C, Q, D values, showing the projection generated, the support grade assigned, and at least one defeater firing. Highest-value single addition.
- **Add a short specification of support grades** (even a three-level sketch and the moves between them), so "lower its support grade" has content.
- **Develop the ordering relation for at least one non-UG target** and connect it explicitly to Goodman's riddle: state what makes the co-availability project rather than merely correlate.
- **Rewrite the profile-specification sentence** to keep the ontic profile distinct from the epistemic specification.
- **Quote Reiss** making the exclusivity claim, or retarget the critique onto the weaker claim he actually makes.
- **Cut/fill the placeholder:** the Acknowledgements still read `[MODEL LIST]` with a TODO (lines 105–106). Fill before any preprint.

### 5. Key question for Q&A

Take one non-UG case, say a register-conditioning claim, and fill in all five elements live: projection target, ordering relation, support grade, scope, defeaters. In particular, **what establishes the ordering relation that makes the conditioning state and the observed forms co-available rather than merely correlated, and what fixes the support grade?** If you can't answer that for a non-UG target as precisely as a Chomskyan can answer "formal dependency" for the grammar-internal one, isn't UG still the only bridge that has actually specified its ordering relation?

### 6. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The framing is right and projectibility genuinely does the framing work rather than decorating a mechanism story, but the apparatus is never instantiated, support-grading and the ordering relation are under-specified, and the load-bearing attribution to Reiss is unverified; all fixable without restructuring.
