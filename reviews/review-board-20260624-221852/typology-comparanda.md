# Review: Typology / Comparative Concepts

**Source-grounding flag.** My deeper claims below about what Haspelmath (2010) and Croft (2001) *commit to* (instrumentalism about comparative concepts; the classification-vs-transfer distinction; RCG's conceptual-space method), and my reference to Corbett-style Canonical Typology, are from my reading of those works, not re-read during this review. They should be verified against the sources before the author relies on them. The paper's one-sentence glosses of Haspelmath and Croft match my memory and are fair.

## 1. One-sentence summary

Crosslinguistic inference requires bridge principles but not Chomskyan Universal Grammar; a projectibility framework (specify target, profile, diagnostics, ordering, stabilizer, scope, defeaters) subsumes UG as one candidate bridge among several, and frameworks like Haspelmath's comparative concepts and Croft's Radical Construction Grammar already supply non-UG bridges supporting a pluralist realism about linguistic targets.

## 2. Strengths

- **The equivocation is cleanly isolated, and the reflexive turn is sharp.** Section 3 ("The Monopoly Claim") names the exact inferential gap, the slide from "languages are comparable / mental grammars are real / primitives are innate" to "*only* UG licenses evidential travel" (line 45), and Sections 1 and 4 turn the screw: Reiss's move from a stipulated `\enquote{linguists}` to "`\term{linguists}` as such" itself "requires the very sort of bridge principle the paper says only UG can provide" (line 29). Using the opponent's own demand reflexively is the strongest thing in the paper.
- **The bridge-principle schema is non-vacuous and has a real adequacy condition.** The S/P/C/Q/D formulation (line 77: "if source features S identify profile P under conditions C, then target expectation Q is licensed until defeaters D appear") plus the self-demotion criterion (line 81: "A serious bridge principle should be able to demote itself") give a testable standard, not a slogan. The Newton point (line 49), that the UG postulate is "asked to do the grouping work, the transfer work, and the exclusionary work at once," is a genuinely good reframing of Reiss's own analogy.
- **The comparanda/realization apparatus is the right tool for a typology audience.** The three-way distinction at line 75 (comparative concept = definition+procedure; comparandum = target pattern; realization = language-internal carrier), with cross-level couplings as "bridge hypotheses ... not identities between levels," is exactly the discipline typologists want and is the paper's most promising original contribution.

## 3. Weaknesses (specific and actionable)

- **The paper names non-UG bridges; it does not show one licensing a crosslinguistic inference.** This is the central problem, and it goes to the question I was asked to probe. Haspelmath gets one sentence (line 71), Croft one sentence (line 73), and neither is ever carried through the paper's own S/P/C/Q/D schema. Not once does the reader see: comparative concept X, source features S in language A, condition C, licensed expectation Q in language B, defeater D. The schema is stated (line 77) and then never instantiated with linguistic content. As it stands the paper asserts that comparative concepts "make comparison accountable" (line 71) without itself rendering a single comparison accountable.
- **The one semi-worked example is intralinguistic, but the burden is crosslinguistic.** The varieties case (line 93, register/dialect/discourse community as conditioning structure) is the closest thing to a demonstration, and it is *within*-language. Yet the entire bridge-principle burden Reiss raises, and that the paper concedes (line 39: "If French data is used to analyse English, or Japanese data is used to analyse English and Icelandic"), is about evidence travelling *across* languages. The paper's only worked illustration is off-target relative to the burden it must discharge.
- **The reflexive demand is stated but not met for the alternatives.** The paper faults UG because "A bridge that never specifies its failure conditions is too permissive to do the work" (line 47), and insists the same discipline applies "reflexively" (lines 59, 95). But it never states the failure conditions of a *comparative concept* on a concrete case. The alternatives are presented exactly as permissively as the UG bridge it criticizes. The paper currently earns, against itself, the charge it lands on Reiss.
- **The Haspelmath appropriation papers over two real tensions.** (a) *Classification vs transfer.* Comparative concepts were designed for crosslinguistic generalization and classification; Haspelmath is famously deflationary about whether one language's descriptive categories license inferences about another's. Reiss's worry is precisely cross-language evidential transfer (French → English). So invoking comparative concepts may *deny that the transfer is licit* rather than *license it*, a different reply to Reiss than the paper thinks it is making, and the paper never distinguishes the two. (b) *Realism.* Haspelmath treats comparative concepts as stipulated tools, not real kinds, which sits awkwardly with the paper's "pluralist realism" that lists "typological comparanda" among targets "real enough for inquiry" (abstract; lines 51, 91). The paper *has* the resource to dissolve (b), its own comparandum-vs-comparative-concept distinction at line 75, but never deploys it on Haspelmath; the two passages sit adjacent and unintegrated.
- **A terminological slip and a placeholder will read as imprecision to typologists.** Line 71 says "the comparandum has to be defined, its diagnostics have to be stated," but by the paper's *own* taxonomy (line 75) it is the comparative *concept* that is "a definition and diagnostic procedure"; the comparandum is "the target pattern," which is not the thing you define. Use "comparative concept" at line 71. Separately, "compared through function and typological patterning" (line 73) leaves "typological patterning" as a placeholder: name the mechanism (conceptual space / semantic maps, implicational hierarchies). Canonical Typology (Corbett and colleagues), the most developed existing method for *accountable* comparison via converging criteria, is a natural ally and a conspicuous omission.

## 4. What to add, cut, or rewrite

- **Add the one thing the paper most needs: a single, compact, fully worked crosslinguistic case.** Take a comparative concept the paper already names (NPI, syllable, or "interrogative element," lines 37 and abstract). Thread it explicitly through S → P → C → Q → D: state the source features in language A, the profile, the conditions, the licensed expectation in language B, and a defeater that would demote or localize the inference. Show it *succeeding* and show it *failing*. This is the difference between naming alternatives and demonstrating them, and it is the make-or-break revision.
- **Add a short passage resolving the Haspelmath tensions.** Distinguish crosslinguistic classification/generalization from cross-language evidential transfer, and say which one your bridge licenses. Then use the line-75 distinction to reconcile "pluralist realism about comparanda" with Haspelmath's instrumentalism about comparative concepts (the worldly *comparandum* can be projectible-hence-real-enough even if the *comparative concept* is a stipulated tool).
- **Rewrite** line 71 ("comparandum" → "comparative concept"); specify "typological patterning" (line 73); consider one sentence recruiting Canonical Typology as a fellow non-UG accountable-comparison method.
- **Cut/consolidate** the thesis refrain. "Reiss's `\enquote{linguists}` can do X for an I-language programme; they haven't shown `\term{linguists}` need UG" lands in near-identical wording at lines 61, 101, and the abstract. Once, sharply, is enough; vary or merge.

## 5. Key question for Q&A

Haspelmath built comparative concepts so that one language's *descriptive categories* do **not** license claims about another language's, they support crosslinguistic generalizations, not cross-language evidential transfer. Reiss's worry is the transfer (French data analyzing English). So when you call comparative concepts "one non-UG bridge" that licenses "evidential travel" (line 71), are you using Haspelmath to *license* that transfer or to *deny it is needed*? Walk one inference, say an NPI diagnosis in language A projecting to an expectation in language B, through your own S/P/C/Q/D schema, and show me exactly where the defeater D lives.

## 6. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The diagnosis of the monopoly fallacy is correct and the projectibility apparatus is a real contribution, but the paper currently *names* Haspelmath and Croft as alternatives without *showing* that either licenses a crosslinguistic inference, so the core promissory note goes unredeemed; one worked case and a resolution of the Haspelmath classification/realism tensions would move it to accept.
