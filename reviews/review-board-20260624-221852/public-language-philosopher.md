# Review: "Bridge Principles without UG"
**Reviewer role:** Public-Language / Philosophy of Language

### Source-grounding caveats
I have not read the cited sources during this review. My judgments about whether the paper is *fair to Reiss* are bounded by not having read `reiss2026armchair`; I take the paper's reconstruction of Reiss at face value. Claims I make below about Stainton, Santana, and the broader public-language literature (Chomsky's E-language critique, Devitt, Rey, Lewis, Ludlow) are from memory and are flagged `[verify]` where they bear weight.

## 1. One-sentence summary

Crosslinguistic inference does require a bridge principle that licenses evidential travel, but Chomskyan UG is only one such licence; a projectibility-based account (projection target, profile, diagnostics, stabilizers, scope, defeaters) licenses comparison across a plurality of real-enough linguistic targets, including public and distributed objects, not just individual mental grammars.

## 2. Strengths

- **The reflexive turn in §4 ("Boundary Stipulation and Disciplinary Ontology") is the paper's best move and it is genuinely sharp.** Turning the bridge-principle demand back on Reiss ("the argument has to explain why conclusions licensed inside an I-language programme project onto corpus linguists, typologists, historical linguists...") is fair, economical, and hard to dodge. The `\enquote{linguists}` vs `\term{linguists}` typographic device makes the equivocation visible rather than merely asserted.

- **The concessive architecture (§2) follows charitable-engagement norms and does real dialectical work.** Granting theory-mediation, realism, armchair method, and the genuine need for a warrant on crosslinguistic evidence narrows the target to the one defensible claim (the *monopoly*), so the paper isn't fighting a strawman. This is the right way to engage Reiss.

- **The Newton analogy in §3 is well-chosen.** The point that universal gravitation "generated equations, measures, counterfactual expectations" while "Reiss's UG postulate is asked to do the grouping work, the transfer work, and the exclusionary work at once" isolates the actual defect (a postulate with no specified failure conditions) cleanly. The "a serious bridge principle should be able to demote itself" criterion in §5 is a good, falsifiable-feeling standard.

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The paper does not make public/distributed targets tractable; it asserts their tractability by analogy.** This is the central problem and it sits squarely in §6. "A public standard... can be real enough for inquiry in the same way that legal systems, currencies, genres, and academic disciplines are real enough: as stabilized objects of coordinated practice." But the Chomskyan critique bites *precisely* on "stabilized objects of coordinated practice." The analogy imports rather than dissolves the problem: legal systems and currencies are tractable largely because they have *explicit institutional individuation and enforcement* (statutes, courts, central banks, legal-tender law). "Standard English" notoriously lacks an analogue. So the analogy, pressed, suggests a *gradient* (institutionalized standards are tractable; emergent vernaculars and registers less so) and thereby cuts against the paper's flat claim that public objects are uniformly "real enough." Either embrace the gradient (and let projectibility grade it) or drop the analogy.

- **§6 never runs the paper's own six-part template on a single public object, even though the Conclusion demands exactly that of everyone else.** The Conclusion's standard is explicit: "say what the target is, which profile carries the inference, which diagnostics identify it, what orders and stabilizes it, where the inference stops, and what would defeat it." §5 instantiates this machinery in detail. §6 substitutes a list of nouns ("maintenance, uptake, correction, transmission, institutionalization, and projectibility") and then says "Those are bridge claims, not slogans." As written, they read as slogans, because the projection target, profile, diagnostics, and defeaters for "Standard English" are never specified. The paper imposes a discipline it does not itself demonstrate on its hardest case.

- **The "misindividuation" charge against Reiss is asserted, not argued, which is ironic given the paper's thesis.** "Reiss's jokes about Standard English and public languages depend on a misindividuation." To establish misindividuation you owe: Reiss's individuation assumption, why it fails, and the correct individuation criterion. The paper gives a genus ("norm-governed, historically maintained, institutionally mediated") but no individuation: *which* institutions, mediated *how*, and crucially, *when do two speakers count as oriented to the same public standard versus different ones?* Without a (defeasible) answer, "misindividuation" is the very kind of unspecified bridge the paper criticizes in Reiss.

- **The paper wins the weaker (terminological-monopoly) battle and sidesteps the stronger (ontological-respectability) one.** "Calling only the first target \enquote{language} doesn't make the other targets disappear" is true but nearly trivial: the Chomskyan claim was never that public objects *vanish*, it is that they are epiphenomenal and do not support projectible generalizations of the kind a science needs `[verify: Chomsky 1986, Knowledge of Language, the E-language-as-epiphenomenal argument]`. The repeated "different inquiries ask different questions" cadence (§6 uses it twice) risks a deflationary reading in which the pluralism is a mere division of labor that no one contests. The paper needs to meet the epiphenomenality charge head-on, not reframe it as a territory dispute over the word "language."

- **The public-language section is thin on the philosophy-of-language literature that would let it make the case rather than assert it.** The Chomskyan side is represented only through Reiss; the realist side only through Stainton and Santana. For a philosophy-of-linguistics audience, the public-language-realism debate has obvious landmarks the paper should be in dialogue with `[verify all]`: Devitt's outputs-based linguistic realism (*Ignorance of Language*, 2006); Rey's eliminativism about linguistic objects; Lewis's conventions account ("Languages and Language," 1975); Ludlow's dynamic-lexicon / microlanguages (*Living Words*) which is strikingly apt for "norm-governed, dynamically maintained" objects; and the Dummett–Chomsky exchange on the priority of public language. Their absence is why §6 reads as gesture rather than position.

- **A latent tension between the Stainton citation and the Neil-Armstrong paragraph.** The paper has Stainton's public language as "a humanly individuated system whose evidence base includes deployment, uptake, and *psychological facts*" `[verify characterization of stainton2011public]`. But the next paragraph wants a thick social-institutional object ("legal systems, currencies"). If the public-language object stays psychologically anchored (Stainton), it drifts back toward the I-language pole and may not deliver the fully distributed object the analogy needs; if it goes fully institutional, Stainton may not license it. The paper appears to conflate two distinct "public language" notions and should pick one or explicitly relate them.

## 4. What to add, cut, or rewrite before a polished full draft

**Add (highest value):**
- A fully worked example in §6 that runs the projectibility template out loud on one named public object (e.g., Standard written English, or a single register): projection target, profile, diagnostics, ordering relation, stabilizers, scope conditions, defeaters. This single addition converts §6 from assertion to demonstration and is the most direct answer to the Chomskyan challenge. The varieties paragraph ("participant expectation conditioned primarily on situation, ascription, or identification") is the seed of this, but it is compressed to one clause and leans entirely on `reynolds2026varieties`; unpack it with one concrete diagnostic and one stated failure condition.
- A defeasible individuation criterion for public-language objects (the currency analogy invites a "shared maintenance institution / shared correction practice" answer; make it explicit).
- Engagement with the epiphenomenality version of the E-language critique and at least one or two of the philosophy-of-language landmarks above, so the realism has a stated home.

**Cut / tighten:**
- The "doesn't make the other targets disappear" line and the second occurrence of "different inquiries ask different questions." They win an uncontested point and make the pluralism look deflationary. Replace with engagement on ontological respectability.

**Rewrite:**
- Recast "misindividuation" as an argument with premises, or soften it to a conditional ("the jokes presuppose an individuation the public-language theorist rejects, because...").
- Reconcile the Stainton sentence with the institutional-object paragraph.

## 5. Key question for Q&A

You require of every bridge principle that it specify projection target, profile, diagnostics, stabilizers, scope, and defeaters. Please run that template, out loud, for "Standard English" as a public-language object. Chomsky's charge was never that public languages *disappear*; it was that they are epiphenomenal and don't support projectible generalizations. If you can complete the template, §6 is finished and you've answered him. If you can't, in what sense have you shown more than that public objects are *describable*, which Reiss never denied?

## 6. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit (leaning positive).** The framing (bridge principles, not boundary policing), the reflexive argument in §4, and the Newton analogy are strong and the core thesis is defensible. But the public/distributed half of the pluralism, which is what earns the paper's "pluralist realism" payoff, is currently asserted by analogy rather than demonstrated with the paper's own machinery, and the philosophy-of-language engagement is too thin to carry §6. These are fixable in revision, not fatal.
