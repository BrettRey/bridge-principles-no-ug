# Review: "Bridge Principles without UG"
**Reviewer persona: Chomskyan Formalist (sympathetic to Reiss)**

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper concedes Reiss's methodological point that crosslinguistic evidence needs a warrant ("bridge principle"), but argues that Chomskyan Universal Grammar is only one such warrant among several, and that a "projectibility-first" framework (specify target, profile, diagnostics, stabilizers, scope, defeaters) better accommodates a pluralist realism about linguistic objects (mental grammars, public standards, corpora, typological comparanda, norm-governed varieties).

## 2. Strengths

- **The concessive structure is genuinely charitable, not performative.** The whole of 搂2 ("What Reiss Gets Right") grants theory-mediation, the non-automaticity of crosslinguistic transfer, and the legitimacy of armchair method as "theory-mediated empirical reasoning." 搂4 explicitly defuses the boundary-policing issue as "methodological rather than personal." This is Rapoport-compliant and it makes the disagreement narrower and more honest than the usual anti-UG broadside. The CLAUDE.md frame ("not a general anti-UG polemic") is respected.

- **The level-discipline is the paper's real contribution.** Distinguishing claims about individual mental grammars from claims about public standards, corpus profiles, typological comparanda, and institutional norms (搂4, 搂6) is exactly the hygiene philosophy of linguistics needs. The "bridge principle as scoped licence" schema in 搂5 ("if source features S identify profile P under conditions C, then target expectation Q is licensed until defeaters D appear") is a clean, falsifiability-respecting formulation that I would happily hold *any* framework to, including my own.

- **The demand for self-application is fair and well-placed.** 搂6's closing point, that public-language arguments must say whether uptake/correction/transmission are "stabilizing backgrounds, parts of the profile, or mere diagnostics," imposes on non-UG objects the same discipline Reiss imposes on crosslinguistic inference. That symmetry is the paper at its best.

## 3. Weaknesses (sharp)

**(a) The central target, the "monopoly claim," is never quoted, and the whole paper rests on it.** The argument depends on attributing to Reiss the claim that "crosslinguistic evidence is licensed *only* by a genetically specified Universal Grammar" (搂1) and that UG is "the only possible bridge" (搂3). I cannot find a single quotation from Reiss in the paper establishing the word that matters: *only*. 搂1 asserts the "monopoly claim"; 搂3 lists five claims and declares "the last claim doesn't follow," but the fifth claim ("only Universal Grammar licenses that evidential travel") is the author's paraphrase, not Reiss's text. A sympathetic reading of Reiss, the one Rapoport's Rules require, is that he is doing *I-language-internal* methodology: within the generative enterprise, comparison presupposes a shared computational system. If that is his actual claim, the monopoly the paper demolishes is the author's construction. **Source-grounding flag: I have not read `reiss2026armchair`; neither, evidently, does the paper let the reader see the decisive passage. This must be fixed with direct quotation or the paper has no target.**

**(b) UG is characterized as a contentless "general postulate," which is a strawman a formalist referee will reject on sight.** Repeatedly the paper calls UG "a general postulate of shared human linguistic endowment" that "doesn't yet specify which inferences are licensed... a projection target, a transfer condition, a scope limit, or a failure mode" (搂1, echoed 搂3). This is false to the actual theory. Substantive UG *is* a theory of transfer conditions with failure modes: subjacency and island effects, the ECP, the binding conditions, the Final-over-Final Condition, head-directionality correlations. These are precisely "scoped licences with defeaters" that travel crosslinguistically and get falsified language by language. The paper attacks bare-postulate-UG while the working licence is substantive-UG. Either the paper equivocates between the two, or it owes an argument that *substantive* UG also fails to specify licences, which it cannot easily make, because substantive UG manifestly does. The Newton analogy in 搂3 cuts *against* the author here: gravitation earned its keep by generating equations and counterfactuals, and that is exactly what generative typology claims UG does. The author cannot grant Newton the contentful version and deny it to UG.

**(c) Once UG is conceded for I-language, the disagreement may dissolve, and the paper doesn't show it survives.** 搂5 concedes "UG can remain a candidate bridge where the projection target is an individual mental grammar"; the Conclusion grants "Reiss's 'linguists' can do that for a Chomskyan I-language programme." 搂4 grants that Reiss's stipulation of "linguists" is "legitimate" and "names a research programme." So: UG is fine for I-language, and Reiss is stipulating I-language inquiry. What is left of the conflict? The paper's answer is that Reiss "sometimes lets a local stipulation do global work" (搂4), but it never quotes the global overreach. A charitable Reiss says "different targets, different bridges; I'm theorizing one target." The author would then be *agreeing with him in a louder voice*. The paper needs to locate a passage where Reiss actually denies that non-I-language linguists have *any* grounds for comparison. Absent that, this is a verbal dispute dressed as a substantive one.

**(d) The "alternatives" beg the question against the formalist.** 搂5 offers Haspelmath's comparative concepts and Croft's Radical Construction Grammar as working non-UG bridges. But these are not neutral: comparative concepts are *stipulated/functional* and are widely argued (the Newmeyer line) to be descriptive conveniences that cannot bear explanatory crosslinguistic weight, and RCG *denies* crosslinguistic syntactic categories by fiat. Citing them as bridges that "work" assumes the functionalist framework is legitimate, which is the very point at issue. The paper asserts these "supply a bridge" (搂5) without showing either one *licenses a specific inference with stated defeaters*, the standard the paper itself sets. **Source-grounding flag: my Newmeyer-vs-Haspelmath characterization is from memory and needs verification before you rely on it; but the burden is yours, the paper must defend these choices, not just name them.**

**(e) No worked example. The paper stays at the meta-level from start to finish.** For a paper whose thesis is that projectibility licenses crosslinguistic inference *better than a bare UG postulate*, there is not one concrete crosslinguistic case carried through the S/P/C/Q/D schema. Not one NPI-licensing comparison, word-order correlation, definiteness profile, or alignment pattern worked from diagnostic to defeater. The varieties example in 搂6 (`reynolds2026varieties`) is the closest, but it is intra-language and gestural. Without a case study, "projectibility-first linguistics" reads as a relabeling of the problem rather than a solution to it, and a formalist will say: this is a frame UG could be *filled into*, not a rival to it. Indeed 搂1 ("Universal Grammar can be part of a proposed support story") concedes exactly that, which contradicts the framing of projectibility as an *alternative* licence in the 搂5 title.

## 4. What to add, cut, or rewrite before this is a polished draft

- **Add the decisive Reiss quotations.** The "only"/monopoly claim (b for the argument) and the alleged slide from stipulated "linguists" to global conclusions (搂4) must appear as quoted text with page numbers. If they cannot be found, the thesis must be reframed from "Reiss overstates" to "the strongest version of Reiss's position would overstate if it claimed X," which is a weaker and more honest paper.
- **Rewrite the UG characterization.** Replace "general postulate of shared endowment" with engagement with substantive UG (islands, binding, parameters). Then make the real argument: that even substantive UG is one bridge among others for I-language *and silent on non-I-language targets*. That argument is winnable; the bare-postulate caricature is not.
- **Settle the rival-vs-frame question.** Decide whether projectibility *competes with* UG or *contains* it, and purge the wavering between 搂1 ("part of a support story") and 搂5 ("an alternative licence"). The containing-frame reading is more defensible and more interesting, but it changes the paper's punch from "Reiss is wrong" to "Reiss is a special case."
- **Add one fully worked crosslinguistic case** run through S鈫扨鈫扖鈫扱鈫扗, ideally one where a UG-style and a non-UG bridge make divergent predictions with stated defeaters. This single addition would do more than any amount of meta-level argument.
- **Defend Haspelmath and Croft** against the standard formalist objection, or drop them as load-bearing and keep them as illustrations.
- **Cut or source the rhetorical characterizations**: "Reiss's jokes about Standard English... depend on a misindividuation" (搂6) and the "grouping/transfer/exclusionary work at once" claim (搂3) read as point-scoring against text the reader cannot see.
- **Reduce self-citation load.** The core apparatus (profile, comparandum/realization, varieties-as-conditioning) rests on three `reynolds` 2025/2026 works. The paper must be self-contained enough that a skeptic without those works can evaluate "profile" and "comparandum."
- **Housekeeping:** the Acknowledgements still contains the literal `[MODEL LIST]` TODO (line 106); and the abstract's "can all be real enough for inquiry when their projection targets and failure conditions are explicit" overclaims relative to a body that shows the conditions *can* be specified, not that they *have been*.

## 5. Key question for Q&A

You concede that UG is a legitimate bridge for I-language inquiry, and you grant that Reiss explicitly stipulates his narrow, Chomskyan sense of "linguists." Can you quote the passage where Reiss claims that *only* UG licenses crosslinguistic evidence, for targets *beyond* the I-language programme he is theorizing? If that monopoly claim is not in his text, what survives of your disagreement beyond the observation, which Reiss could cheerfully accept, that different linguistic targets call for different bridges?

## 6. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The level-discipline and the concessive structure are real strengths and the position is defensible, but the paper demolishes a monopoly claim it never quotes and a "bare-postulate" UG no formalist holds, and it offers no worked case showing projectibility does what UG allegedly cannot; supply the Reiss text, engage substantive UG, and add one carried-through example, and this becomes publishable.
