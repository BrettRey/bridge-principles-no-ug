## Review: "Bridge Principles without UG" — Hostile Methodologist

**Source-grounding caveat up front:** I have not read `reiss2026armchair`, `haspelmath2010`, `croft2001`, or the three Reynolds entries during this review. Where I make claims about what Reiss or Haspelmath actually says, treat them as flagged for verification. That limitation is itself relevant to weakness W1 below.

### 1. One-sentence summary

The paper concedes Reiss's point that crosslinguistic evidence needs a licensing "bridge principle," but argues that Chomskyan Universal Grammar is only one such licence among several, and offers a projectibility-based schema (target, profile, diagnostics, stabilizers, scope conditions, defeaters) as a pluralist alternative that admits public standards, corpora, typological comparanda, and varieties as real targets of inquiry.

### 2. Strengths

- **The "too unspecific to license" objection is genuinely good (§§1, 3).** The claim that a bare postulate of shared endowment "doesn't identify a projection target, a transfer condition, a scope limit, or a failure mode" is a real methodological point, not a rhetorical one. A licensing principle that never states its failure conditions cannot discriminate warranted from unwarranted transfers, and "A bridge that never specifies its failure conditions is too permissive to do the work Reiss assigns to it" (§3) is a fair, sharp formulation.
- **The Newton analogy is turned against Reiss correctly (§3).** Showing that universal gravitation "generated equations, measures, counterfactual expectations, and conditions under which the theory would be strained" — and that a UG postulate asked to do "the grouping work, the transfer work, and the exclusionary work at once" does none of these — is a legitimate disanalogy, well placed.
- **The paper holds its own alternatives to the standard it imposes on Reiss (§6).** "A public-language argument has to say which role they play... it imposes the same discipline on non-UG objects that Reiss rightly imposes on crosslinguistic inference." This reflexive honesty is rare and raises the paper's credibility above the typical anti-UG reply.

### 3. Weaknesses (sharp)

- **W1 — The target ("the monopoly claim") is never documented with a quotation.** The entire paper rests on attributing to Reiss the strong claim that "crosslinguistic evidence is licensed *only* by a genetically specified Universal Grammar" (§1; restated §3 as "only Universal Grammar licenses that evidential travel"). Not one direct quotation from Reiss is given to establish that he holds this in the strong "only" form, rather than the weaker "UG is *a* sufficient bridge" or "crosslinguistic comparison presupposes shared endowment." A methodologist cannot grant that the refutation hits a real position. As written, the paper is vulnerable to the reply "Reiss never claimed monopoly; you built it." This is the single most serious problem and it is purely a sourcing fix: quote the monopoly claim, in its strongest available form, verbatim.

- **W2 — The projectibility account is a schema that never once runs on a real inference.** Across six sections the paper repeats the list of questions projectibility "can ask" — target, profile, diagnostics, ordering relation, stabilizers, scope conditions, defeaters (§§1, 5, 7) — but never works a single concrete crosslinguistic case through it. The paper's own §2 examples (using Japanese data to analyze English and Icelandic) are exactly what should be carried through the schema to a determinate verdict. "Projectibility-first linguistics can ask the missing questions directly" (§3) is an IOU. Asking is not answering. No diagnostic is ever named, no profile is ever specified for an actual category, no defeater is ever instantiated. The abstract promises an account that says "where the projection should be localized, demoted, or rejected"; the body localizes, demotes, or rejects nothing.

- **W3 — The framework may rule nothing out (falsifiability/relabeling worry).** This is the deep one. The schema is general enough that *anything*, including UG, can be redescribed in it: "UG can remain a candidate bridge where the projection target is an individual mental grammar and the proposed stabilizing support is a genetically constrained language faculty" (§5). If every proposed bridge — UG included — is expressible as a profile with stabilizers and defeaters, then projectibility is not a *rival licence*; it is a *notation* into which all parties translate. The paper needs at least one case where the projectibility account delivers a determinate verdict (licenses X, blocks Y) that the UG account does not, or gets a case demonstrably right that UG gets wrong. Relatedly, the paper invokes graded support ("lower its support grade," "support grade," §5) with no scale defined — what are the grades, how is support measured? Without a discriminating verdict and a defined support scale, the contribution is terminological, not methodological.

- **W4 — Possible conflation of "comparison" with "evidential travel," which sinks the counterexamples.** Reiss's burden, as the paper states it, is about *evidential transfer*: whether "French data is used to analyse English" is justified (§§2, 3). But the three offered non-UG bridges address *commensurable description*, not transfer. Haspelmath's comparative concepts are, on the paper's own gloss, "constructed for crosslinguistic comparison rather than discovered as a universal category" (§5) — analyst's yardsticks, on a famously deflationary/instrumentalist reading of Haspelmath. A common yardstick lets you *describe* L1 and L2 commensurably; it does not obviously *license using L1 data as evidence for an L2 claim*. There is also a tension worth confronting: the paper wants "pluralist *realism*" (abstract), yet recruits its first two bridges from frameworks that are instrumentalist about comparanda (Haspelmath) and anti-universalist about categories (Croft). If the bridges are deflationary, they may not deliver the realism the thesis advertises. (Flag: this rests on my background reading of Haspelmath 2010, not a re-read here — verify.)

- **W5 — The equivocation charge is undocumented and partly self-undermining.** The paper grants Reiss's stipulation — "Reiss's restriction is best granted rather than resisted" (§4) — and then complains that "the paper sometimes lets a local stipulation do global work" (§4). But "sometimes" cites no instance, and if Reiss explicitly restricts "linguists" to the Chomskyan programme, then conclusions about ⟨linguists⟩ are, by his own lights, scoped to ⟨linguists⟩ and there is no equivocation to expose — only a limited-scope conclusion. To make the charge stick, the paper must quote a place where Reiss deploys a ⟨linguists⟩-scoped premise to a ⟨linguists⟩-as-the-field conclusion (e.g., that non-Chomskyans "lack grounds for comparison"). Otherwise §4 alleges a fallacy it does not catch in the act.

- **W6 (lesser) — The "misindividuation" rebuttal renames rather than argues (§6).** "Reiss's jokes... depend on a misindividuation" is followed by reclassifying the standard as "real enough... in the same way that legal systems, currencies, genres, and academic disciplines are real enough." Four heterogeneous social objects are lumped without showing they share the relevant individuation properties, and Reiss has presumably heard the "languages are like currencies" move and has a reply. The paper owes the argument, not the list.

### 4. What to add, cut, or rewrite before this is a polished full draft

- **Add (non-negotiable): at least one verbatim quotation establishing the monopoly claim** in its strongest form (W1). Without it the paper has no documented target.
- **Add (non-negotiable): one fully worked crosslinguistic case** carried end-to-end through the schema — projection target, named diagnostics, the profile, the ordering relation, the stabilizer, and an actual defeater that would localize/demote/reject the transfer (W2). Use the paper's own Japanese→English/Icelandic example. One worked case does more than the current six sections of schema.
- **Add: a discrimination test** — a single inference the projectibility account licenses that UG does not, or vice versa, with the verdict shown (W3). This is what converts "new vocabulary" into "new method."
- **Rewrite §5 to separate "comparison" from "evidential travel"** and show that Haspelmath/Croft license the latter, not just the former — or concede they license commensurable description and find a bridge that actually licenses transfer (W4).
- **Rewrite §4** to quote the equivocation in Reiss, or downgrade the charge from "fallacy" to "the conclusion is narrower than its rhetoric suggests" (W5).
- **Cut or defend §6's four-object analogy;** define "support grade" if the graded-support language stays (W3, W6).
- **Housekeeping:** the `[MODEL LIST]` placeholder and TODO in Acknowledgements (lines 105–106) must be filled before circulation.

### 5. Key question for Q&A

Take one actual inference from Reiss's own examples — say, using Japanese data to draw a conclusion about English syntax — and run it completely through your projectibility schema to a determinate verdict. Name the diagnostics, the profile, and the specific defeater. Then tell us: what does that verdict rule *out* that Reiss's UG account does not? If UG itself is just "a candidate bridge" in your vocabulary (§5), what crosslinguistic transfer does projectibility *block* that UG *permits*?

### 6. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.** The thesis is real, the structure is sound, and the "underspecification" and reflexive-discipline points are genuine contributions — but the paper currently refutes an unquoted target (W1) and demonstrates its alternative on zero worked cases (W2), so its central claim that projectibility *does work UG cannot* is asserted, not shown. It is one good worked example away from acceptable and one unanswered Q&A question away from reject; I hold genuine uncertainty about which way it breaks.
